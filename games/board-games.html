Board Games
This is a page for two board games I worked on during my graduate study.
"Dig!" is a game I crafted together with Michael Miller and Zach Van Cleef with a theme of paleontology.
"Cosmos" is a board game I created for the course project for Stone Librande's Game Design Fundamentals course. It is an 1v1v1 resource management game where players compete to be the first person to obtain enough score to win.
time spring 2020, spring 2021
genre board game
length 15-20 minutes
work co-designer (Dig!), individual work (Cosmos)

Brainstorming
Michael, Zach and I were assigned a couple of scientific fields to use as the foundation for our game. We chose anthropology and paleontology after some research and discussions, and created two fictional people for each science to help with further brainstorming. Brainstorming Doc
We as a team also created a game pitch slide deck for in-class presentation. Game Pitch Slides As we moved forward, we narrowed down the ideas and chose to go with what I pitched inside the team: to play as a group of paleontological fossil monitors who are on active duty in a national park, and to venture out into hazardous weather to protect and preserve fossils.
Gameplay
collaboration
As we were confirming the theme of our board game, I suggested that the game will fit a collaborative mechanism where players work together to save fossils. The collaboration also enables another gameplay we wanted to implement in the game: player abilities. Each player will have a unique ability that gives them an edge over standard rules.
tile-based map
Zach and Michael brought up the idea for the map: the hex tiles have paths on one side, while the other side that can be flipped, contains either a fossil or nothing. We wanted to combine the strategy of choosing paths with the excitement of flipping tiles. This is also a smart way to implement the story that fossils are buried deep underground and the difficulties to uncover them.
weather
Another major gameplay point I created was the weather cards. In the game, weather cards are drawn and revealed at the end of each round, and are designed as a method to add special player-game interactions that brings a little chaos and challenges. The weather system also reflects the harsh uncertainty of outdoor paleontological activities.
Playtesting
We've been conducting extensive playtests to polish the game. When playtesting we specifically asked questions on design choices we wanted to test out, and made changes based on the analysis of player report. I will put playtest reports as a link here later when they are all finished.
Game 2 - Cosmos
Link to the game on Tabletop Simulator
Researching
I initially decided to try making a competitive deck-building game. I started the ideation phase by studying similar games that have proven commercially and critically successful. After that, I outlined some of the key elements that I would love to implement in my own game.
Slay the Spire
Slay the Spire is a deck-building roguelike made by Mega Crit. A single player game, Slay the Spire features four playable characters, each of whom has their unique deck where many examples can be found for learning card synergy and balancing. What's more, Slay the Spire also does a good job in balancing the skill and luck factor using procedurally generated maps and semi-randomly dropped cards and power-ups as the base of its replayability. There's the link to my detailed analysis of the game: Slay the Spire: Engagement and Balance.
Star Realms
Star Realms is a deck-building board game pitting players against each other in a fight for dominance over the entire galaxy. A multiplayer card game with four Factions (camps), Star Realms does a good job designing the deck-building mechanism by offering a shared trade row, fair enough for both sides. The game uses a very straight forward way to make sure players pick the cards of same Faction: the powerful Ally ability can only be triggered if the player have another card of that faction in-play.
Outlining
backstory
A star in a faraway galaxy is to explode in supernova soon, which will kill every living thing in its range. Three civilizations embark on the great exodus:
Red civilization is aggressive and always wants to wipe out other civilization in the galaxy;
Yellow civilization is successful colonizing and works closely among multiple stars;
Blue civilization never lacks scientists and philosophers because they are so eager to learn new knowledge.
gameplay overview
Cosmos is a 1v1v1 turn-based deck-building card game where each player tries to move their mothership to the destination point on the map. The game offers ways to move your own mothership and also to impede other motherships' moving process. The ship's movement is similar to the health in many competitive games, but with real blocks on the map more gameplay tricks can be designed.
game components
The game uses cards and tokens.

The cards are in 3 colors: blue, red and yellow, each standing for one civilization. Each civilization has their unique cards that suits its background. Each player has a Mothership which needs to move to the destination to win. There are three basic collections of ships:
Explorers: The units mainly for getting resources on the map.
Fighters: The units that have diverse abilities from boosting own camp's mothership to impeding other camps, for instance, moving their Motherships back or destroying ships.
Boosters: The units that mainly move the Mothership.

Tokens will be put on the map to represent all the units and Motherships.
Alpha: Gameplay
cards
There are two kinds of units: Ships are ones that can move by itself (usually determined by a 6-sided die), and Boosters are ones attached to the Mothership but can make the Mothership move.
Some ships can gain money from the map (indicated by the numbers in blocks) and buy new units from a shared trade list.
map
The first version is a 1v1 game with a map where each player owns a Mothership that needs to be moved from the start point to the destination. The map has 50 blocks so in most circumstances the Mothership needs to move 50 blocks.
Problems
The first prototype only has two camps and a relatively limited number of cards, despite of which I still managed to figure out a bunch of problems on balance, game feeling and map.
Balance: It turns out some cards are OP while some are almost useless. Balancing cards is simple as the major work is tweaking the value of each card. But when it comes related to feeling and pace, changing card becomes much harder.
Game feeling: I put card's synergy and their unique abilities on high priority. During playtesting, some says they really like the creative design of cards, but other also say that it is hard to understand the rules and playing too many cards in a single turn takes time and energy. It is especially true in late games as the rules allow triggering every ability in a single turn which can cause chaos and difficulty.
Pace: The game feels too slow but once someone gets a key card it can get ridiculously fast. Evidently some cards are OP.
Beta: Camps
card types
I want to clarify card's types so as to balance some card's abilities and make it feel more natural because there are basically two types of movement on the map, the first being ships that can move by themselves, and the second are boosters attached to the Mothership and move with it.

In beta version I concluded three camps' keywords and used them as the principle for designing cards.
red camp
Keyword: Glory, Sacrifice
Red camp is warlike. Many red units can apply negative effects on the opponent’s units to fuel the battle.
Sacrifice: Some units can sacrifice itself for the greater merit.
blue camp
Keyword: Synergy, Control
Blue camp puts order at a very important place, so they have strong card synergy that can control the battlefield and change the battle.
Control: Disable target ship’s abilities.
yellow camp
Keyword: Evolution, Telekinesis
The yellow camp is more into physiological and personal evolution. Their cards are mostly expensive, but with very unique abilities such as telekinesis that can teleport Mothership, Yellow camp has the highest flexibility in late game and could dominate the game once its galactic fleet arrives.
Telekinesis: Move target ship or Mothership instantly to a target location.
card balance
Balancing has been a major problem for many card games and especially deck-building games. Since the beta version is pretty rough with almost no testing, I want to try establishing a mathematical model for cards' costs, values and special abilities so I can stick to the pattern and avoid making big mistakes.
Final: A Tough Pivoting
The main reason for the pivoting was the balancing issue and the workload of a deck-building game that went beyond my estimation. Despite many cool ideas that came up to my mind, I realize that the game I was building was still far from well-rounded and I had to spent a lot of time to tweak the value.

So I decided to make a simplified game with the space theme kept. I was thinking of a resource management game then because I as well had rich experiences playing some typical resource management game such as Catan and Splendor.
Top-down Design
I still wanted to keep the competitive gameplay and wanted to use an 1v1v1 mechanism to trigger some interesting interactions between players, because 1v1v1 is usually not an "equal" situation as two weaker players could ally up against the strongest player. After a month's playtesting and iterating, I finally settled down upon the final playable version. Here's the link to the rulebook.
