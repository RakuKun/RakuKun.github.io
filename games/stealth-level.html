Stealth Level Design
I chose to build up a stealth level as my first work with Unreal Engine. In this project I tried to answer three core questions: What a stealth level flow should be like? How to use visual elements to guide the player? How to design a level with limited interior space?
time summer 2020
length ~ 10 minutes
software Unreal Engine 4, Maya, Illustrator
work individual work
Stealth Design Theory
Stealth game became a serious game genre after Metal Gear Solid came out. Its third person perspective gameplay, combined with dramatic plot and the thrilling feeling taking down enemies, fascinates players who want something different from a shoot-'em-up.
element analysis
From the experience of several stealth games I want to analyze what makes them fun and try making a brief stealth level by myself. Just like other games, there are some fundamental elements in stealth games: a start, a goal, game rules, and obstacles.
start
In stealth games, usually, the start point is a relatively safe place distant from destination. No challenges at the beginning means players have sufficient time to get prepared such as grabbing some equipments and inspecting the level to make a plan. To make the game more challenging, the players may not be able to come back once they set off, so each run needs to be well prepared.
goal
The goal in stealth game is very diverse but has something in common: mostly some fixed points (treasure, exit, important person, etc.) Some games would ask the players to escape once they reach the goal (kill a person probably), and the game pace could change a lot in the escape phase, which adds excitement and up & downs to the emotion flow.
rules
The principle of designing rules for stealth games is never to make the playable character too strong that they can even confront a group of armed enemies. Giving ability to engage is dangerous in the sense that a stealth experience may turn into Call of Duty. As a compensation, the character is offered some skills or items so they can pass through enemies unnoticed.
obstacles
There are usually a group of enemies either in fixed points or wandering certain routes, trying to find and catch the playable character. They outnumber the character so it is hard once got into combat, but for a playable level their actions must be predictable so the players can have strategy and plan ahead.
pitfalls
god path
The god path is a route by which the character have zero chance of being noticed. It usually happens when all enemies' patrol routes don't overlap with the character's route. This bad design could make the level very boring as the players don't need to think, they just go and win.

Another example of god path is the unfair vantage point, where players are allowed to attack enemy from distance while they cannot fight back. Again, every game need to balance its challenge and reward. God path avoids all challenges, thus causing a broken game setup.
unpredictable AI
Unpredictable AI means pre-scripted enemies don't follow a fixed path, or have sudden turn back, or moves too fast. Unpredictable AI makes the game hard in an annoying but not challenging way, as the random events would kill every plan and mess up the level.
forced gameplay
Many stealth games let playable character have items or weapons. This is nice as the players are granted a variety of ways to beat the level. But this could also be a pain when some items are designed very specifically for one level or even one point in level, and the players cannot beat the level if they don't have that. It is always better to provide possibility for trying new ways to explore than limiting the gameplay to one answer.
game reference
Splinter Cell: Blacklist
Splinter Cell: Blacklist is the stealth game that I'm mostly getting inspired from, and this stealth level project is like the re-creation of one of its levels call Private Estate. A scenario set at night, Private Estate serves as a great example of the use of light, enemy AI, and exterior and interior design of a typical stealth level.
MGS V: Phantom Pain
Metal Gear Solid V: Phantom Pain is a critically claimed stealth title from one of my favorite game director, Hideo Kojima. I like its day-night system, which adds different fun and strategy to a single map. Again, MGSV teaches a good lesson on how crucial lighting in dark scenes.
Uncharted 4
Although Uncharted 4 is not a stealth game, some of its levels cleverly add stealth element to make the game feel diverse and interesting. What's more, the way it tells a continuous story using level design is a great source for learning. The picture above comes from "get out of the orphanage" scenario in chapter 1, which shows the players a segment of Nathan Drake's childhood.

This level did another point extremely well: make waiting time interesting. Usually in stealth levels players need to wait for enemies to pass or turn around, and the process can be tedious. Uncharted 4, however, makes NPCs talk and act all the time, so that players can concentrate and get information useful to understand the story. It's indeed a clever move.

layout reference
When designing, I studied pictures of many real world mansions to better understand the layout.
2d layout
During design phase I used Adobe Illustrator to block out the level and use some legends for the playable character, enemies and goal. I would like to make 2 major scenes in the game: the outdoor scene and the indoor scene. The first goal is set outside the mansion while the second goal needs the character to sneak into the mansion and stealth in a more constraint area.
Process
methodology
macro -> micro
I like to ponder the design from a macro perspective first, then zoom in on the details. For this project, the game pace is the first thing confirmed. When I was drawing the 2d map, I always design the large part first (exterior & interior), then split each part into smaller pieces (garage/living room/bedroom), then decide where to put the lighting, then consider level dressing such as furniture.
3d modelling
Putting 2D maps into a 3D world is not simply replacing lines with cubes. I need to think how to translate the design faithfully and efficiently:
-Pay special attention to the height difference. Height difference would matter a lot in determining the visibility, and engaging enemies from a higher place is possible but is almost impossible from a lower place.
-Lines in 3D world have height and thickness. Wall is a key component in 3D stealth games as it blocks the sight and gives space for players to hide and ambush.
level design
mesh & color
Although the purpose of this level is not to make an environment design portfolio, I built the level in medium details, especially for places that should attract more attention. Coloring the mesh is also important, as it gives a sense of what function the mesh serves.

What is interesting is that, when I was blocking out the level and testing, I found in real games, each floor in interior levels should better be HIGHER than in real life, due to fact that the third-person camera angle is usually higher than the player character. Translucent material is another level component I played with this time. I used it to make the mansion feel modern and living, also to help players see through some part of the mansion and guide them.
lighting
Lighting plays an significant role in night scenes. In the level lighting was used mostly to indicate important locations and direct the players. I tried to use different colors for different purposes: yellow means common places, white can be seen at the entrance of large area, light yellow is used for enemies' flashlights.
collectibles
Collectibles are commonly used in games to reward those who have curiosity and patience to explore every corner of the world. To fill the world with more content, I also use collectibles to enrich some not very eye-catching routes.
level construction
I used Blueprint to do all the game logic, including enemy AI, patrolling system, and game start/over. And it's also a step where I can watch how level runs and adjust accordingly. The best part of all these systems is their compatibility - Unreal Engine makes it easy to transplant the systems to another game that needs them.
patrol system
enemy AI
To create a highly customizable and robust AI system, I used a combination of Unreal Engine's AI perception system, behavior tree, and various Blueprints. Also to increase the visibility of AI sight, I used enemies' flashlights to indicate their sight range, which proved to be working well in playtests.
I think in a dynamic point of view. Enemies have vision cones and as they move, they are observing different places. As I programmed the whole game system, there are way more details that needs careful thoughts.
dialogue system
Dialogue system in this game also plays as a guide that reminds the player of where they are and what they should be doing.
playtesting
I consider playtesting a crucial part of level design, especially when I realized the magic it plays to bring a 6/10 level to 8 points. (or higher!)
The first feedback from a professional includes changing the position of vantage point and adding more direction along the level so the player can keep track of where they are and know where is the destination. I also noticed that enemy patrol routes are so easy that there's actually "god path" which is mentioned earlier as a pitfall in stealth level. As a result, I re-designed exterior area and enemy patrol routes.
Post-Mortem
what went well
atmosphere
Almost every playtesters mentioned the intense atmosphere when playing, which quite fits my expectation.
using the engine
Prior to the project (which is my first official UE project), I spent about two weeks learning some basic stuff about Unreal Engine, all of which eventually proved useful.
what didn't went well
interior design
Admittedly, designing indoor level almost took me twice the time of outdoor part. I realized the composition became really different when I wanted the guide the players in a narrow area. And setting obstacles was also more difficult when designing the inside part.
missing details
Yes, I talked to myself and found that compared to enhancing the details, I was more willing to think about the macro - feeling, level flow, constructing logic... The level was definitely not perfect as I sensed many details are still missing - and some of them might have been a good guide or challenge.
