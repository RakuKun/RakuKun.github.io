Project Ditto
Ditto is a collaboration project between Google Stadia and the ETC (Silicon Valley Campus) where I worked in a great team of five developing multiplayer game prototypes for Stadia platform. (title picture by Jue Wang)

The team members are: Liam Philiben, Jue Wang, Siyu (Tracy) Chen, Wenyu Mao, and me.

We've built a project website. I've also written design documentation to record design process and takeaways, check it here: online design documentation.
time spring 2020
platform Unity, Stadia
work game/level design, modelling
Background
Stadia is a cloud gaming service operated by Google, advertised to be capable of streaming high-quality video games to players via the company's numerous data centers across the globe. The service is accessible through most smart ends like cell phones and PC, provided the users are using a sufficiently high-speed Internet connection.

In March 2019, Stadia announced a platform exclusive feature called State Share, which leverages cloud game hosting to enable players to share a complete game state (not just a specified save state) instantly to others. The goal for project Ditto is to explore the possibility of this feature specifically for the discovery of new kinds of game design patterns and new game genres that are inherently viral and require to elaborate player cooperation across massive scale audience.
Pre-production
Pre-production witnessed our first two months during which we spent about three weeks doing initial research and pitching ideas in the ideation phase, and the rest five weeks developing paper and digital prototypes.
researching
clarifying path
As State Share is a relatively new technology with little reference and tech demo publicly published, we started off by thinking the special points of State Share and how it enables the potential the game design.

After reading the post on Stadia's developer website ,we concluded some of its merits:
1. State Share integrates well with Youtube streaming as it allows viewers to jump into the same game as the streamers easily. This broadens the way streamer and viewer can interact.
2. State Share create the link along with last 30 seconds' gameplay, and all information during that time will be included. The developers have much freedom to decide which and how the information is to be used.
3. State Share works with other Stadia features such as Crowd Play, and Stadia team is working to bring more flexibility to its function.

What's more, during discussions with our client, Erin Hoffman-John, we were assured that for most tech issues we could "fake" State Share in Unity without really implementing the Stadia SDK. In that sense, we as developers could concentrate on creating a game mechanism that centers State Share as core gameplay.
ideation
After internal and client meetings we concluded ten possible ways of implementing State Share, with a total over 30 different ideas.
We realized that we had too many ideas all of which seemed plausible. Our next move was to narrow down ideas into several representative ones each catagories its type. This time we got five ideas with more concrete gameplay, ready for prototyping.
prototyping
paper prototype
We each created one paper prototype from the five ideas as designing on paper could expose the core mechanics well. The one I designed, "Bomb Express" explores the concept "delivering the state".
We realized that State Share serves better as an "amplifier" for the existing experience, while it does offer new possibilities for multiplayer mechanics.
playtests
It is also very important to run playtests as many as possible. For paper prototypes we have run 3 rounds of playtests, the first and second being internal testing, and the third time we invited some EA individuals whose experience is very valuable to improve our design. Here are some suggestions from the playtesters:
1. Think of State Share as a core feature without which the gameplay would change a lot.
2. Be careful about the theme: is the fiction suitable for all ages? Will the game mechanism ask for some private information such as living location?
3. Some of our paper prototypes have issues on replayability as they are puzzle driven. This can be difficult as State Share is designed for a relatively large number of people so spoilers may have an impact on the game.
4. Online multiplayer games are challenging but seems more promising, while unsynchronized gameplay is easier to do digital prototypes.
learnings
From feedbacks, follow-up discussions and client meetings, we have drawn up three design criteria:
- Asymmetrical gameplay is suitable for streamer and audience and well conveys State Share’s idea.
- Having a persistent world (like MMO games) is state sharable and could let players feel more immersed.
- To make a multiplicative distribution rather than transferring a single branch to new players.

Now that we were pretty clear about how to keep moving forward, we stepped into the digital prototyping phase.
digital prototype
We built a digital prototype for the Virus vs. Cell theme which utilized State Share well as the core mechanism - without State Share the game will be unplayable.

My major job was designing and modelling levels for the prototype. I spent some time researching levels in LocoRoco for inspiration.
moving forward
While developing the digital prototype, we came up with another paper prototype: growing a treehouse. After discussing with our client, we realized that we are in fact building two types of games, the first one being skill-based game (Virus vs. Cells), and the second one being expressive game (treehouse). Having no more than eight weeks for developing a high fidelity prototype, we wanted to move forward with the Virus vs. Cell one since we believed it had a more feasible scope. This did not mean the time spent on treehouse prototype was meaningless: we recorded it in the design documentation in case anyone is interested in the future.
Production and Pivoting
The second half of the semester was pretty dramatic: we experienced some setback adjusting the project to work-from-home style, but were also granted a chance to pivot the game's theme and gameplay. The final product is called: Immunoo!
Changing to PVE
We experienced a pivoting in the foci of project soon after the second half of the semester began. From then on, the game would focus on its PVE content rather than PVP, asking designers to come up with new ways to let players call their friends to join the game. The original theme, Virus vs. Cell remained intact, but we did want the players to play as the guarding cells in the human body to experience the excitement and sense of achievement while fighting the evil, chaotic viruses.

During internal meetings, we realized the limitation in time and decided that we should start from a single scene, a single type of player, and a single type of enemy: the player will act as white blood cell trying to wipe out viruses inside a man's heart, the feeling of gameplay should be cute and dangerous.

As the game and level designer on the team, after determining several environmental effects in the level, I started working on the level layout.
Level Design
level mechanics
Since the game happens inside the heart, we wanted to use some unique environmental mechanics to propel the players into challenges. Then the rhythm bloodstream came to mind: both the virus and the white blood cell will move with the blood stream. However, the virus will be largely influenced by the blood flow, while the white blood cell will have some control over its movement. This could make the "chase" part fun and strategic, and made controlling the level flow easier.

The game should still highlight the interaction between players because they now can see each other in the game, and fit the spirit of State Share.
level layout
Inspired by our client’s suggestion, we use a method called IPM (Introduce, Practice, Master) as a principle in designing the levels. All chambers are designed for multiplayer experience, with increasing demands for skill mastery from players. The increasing difficulty mainly means more enemies and more complex mechanics in the later chambers. The following shows the final level layout:
The last chamber was designed to be the boss fight room, with a preparation room in front of it which technically serves as a place for players to invite their friends/audience and team up before entering the boss room.

The boss fight’s mechanics are partly different from previous experience because we wanted to make it feel more intense and highlight the connection between each player and the necessity of sharing and helping others. The powerful mother virus is in invulnerable at the beginning, players will have to eat all the surrounding child viruses in order to make it vulnerable and surround it again to disintegrate and “kill” it.
Level Dressing
As the 3D artist, I was responsible for creating all 3D assets for the level. Working with 2D artist, I converted what was believed as "cuteness" to 3D, bringing a unified art style for the game.
I used Blender to prepare 3D models.
Future Updates
The team has thought over the future updates for the game if possible. We created different environment effects, enemies, unique skills that can be applied to different parts of human body.
Post-Mortem
This is my first client project at the ETC. The project involved lots of researching, prototyping, and rounds of iteration, which I enjoyed doing as the process always inspires me to be a better game designer.

The project was not flawless, of course. We could have done more if the schedule was not interrupted by the outbreak, and we lost some productivity due to work-from-home. Many features we designed were still on the paper because we didn't have time to implement them.
