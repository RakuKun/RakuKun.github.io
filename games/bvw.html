Building Virtual Worlds
Building Virtual Worlds is a class in the first semester of Master of Entertainment Technology Program where I in teams of five take part as game designer and artist to make quick game prototypes on mostly non-PC platform. Each game takes one to three weeks.

Gameplay
team setup
Two players controls a firetruck to fight the other two players. In a team, one person drives the firetruck and can press physical button to perform a dash; the other person controls the direction of water cannon and can press the button to spray water to knock the other firetruck back.
map
The map is a round-shaped island whose 3 outer rings will fall during the game. There is a center fire hydrant which sprays powerful water.
energy system
Every player has energy that replenishes automatically every 2 seconds. Pressing the button will use all the current energy and make the firetruck dash forward/spray water according to the role. The game will be randomly spawning powerups on the map that replenish the energy bar to full immediately.
game end
A game ends when one firetruck gets off the ground for any reasons, by which the opposite side wins. Every match is best of three so losing one game doesn't mean losing it all.
Design Elements
asymmetrical role
One player drives the firetruck, another controls the water cannon. By assigning different abilities we make players stay connected to each other and communicate along the game.
controlled chaos
We love to have a little chaos in the game as randomness is an easy way to add fun. However, it also needs control so that winning the game is not totally by luck. In our game we have designed two semi-random events:
1. The central fire hydrant will spray water every fixed time, but randomly at some of 4 directions.
2. Overtime powerups will be spawned on the map, and we control the distribution and kind so one team will not continuously luck out.
In a word, we don't like 100% skill or 100% luck game. The randomness must be well controlled to not overwhelm players’ performance.
limited gametime
Playing such fast paced game is physically and mentally challenging so we want each round no longer than 2 minutes.
The island rings fall and battleground gets smaller overtime, making the battle more intense and game end more quickly.
Post-Mortem
clearer instructions
Before putting our game to BVW Festival there was almost no tutorial so we have to tell players manually how to play and win the game, and sometimes delivering rules orally could cause misunderstanding. So I make two slides for instructions and show them on a TV screen near Jam-O-Drum.
balance
Most players claim that driving the firetruck feels actually harder than spraying water. It is not a bad thing to have a diverse role difficulty inside a team, but we need to clarify it in the first place or players might get frustrated for underperforming.

Story
easy story
It will be a 4 minute experience including interaction time, so we don't want a too complicated story. We choose to make a classroom romance story since it is a universally common theme easy for everyone to understand.
meaningful story
We get advice from professors that to make the story truly convincing, every step should have a motivation. Why is this day so important that the character is daydreaming about his girl? Why does the girl accept the love letter?
We answer these questions by putting a calendar in the start scene, indicating that they already know each other, and the boy waits for this day to profess his love to her. To make a complete story, we have the same scene (at night) in the ending, with changes on the calendar.
Additionally, we think adding a little surprise in the story would help the emotional flow, while the story shouldn't break main story trend. So we introduces a super handsome guy seemingly coming out of nowhere just to win the girl, yet his prompt actually gets declined.
interactive story
What Remains of Edith Finch inspires me most in combining interactions with story. We leave important moments waiting to be accomplished by player so they feel really being in the situation rather than watching passively the story happen.
Another thing from this game that hits me is the use of metaphor. What Remains of Edith Finch uses visual languages to imply psychological activities, and I want to use the same to represent love. So in our game we have scenes jumping between dreams and reality, with the huge difference in tones and actions to show the character's love for the girl.
Design Process
idea iteration
We spent much time iterating each story and interaction. Below are some very early-stage idea sketches.
After determining the theme, we started off ideation by thinking of activities that best represent school love. I come up with ideas using metaphor mentioned above to create an illusion-esque feeling of love, and we together designed several actions that is reasonable in the classroom and suitable for VR.
During the interim the majority of feedback we got was that the interaction is cool, but many don't quite understand the story and the ending (a handsome guy breaks in and takes the girl) is quite frustrating. So we make the start scene and redo the ending to make it kind of more conservative but reasonable.
Post-Mortem
deliver more clearly
Some players have a hard time figuring out how the story and interaction is connected. And it is hard to have the same feeling as What Remains of Edith Finch as we are short on art and sound resources. So it might be better if we deliver the interaction in a clearer way with less metaphors.
do playtests many as possible
More playtesters help more in finding bugs and testing the interactions. If they don’t understand, that is probably we are making it too complicated. This doesn't necessarily mean that we need to cut the story to cater for everyone's taste, but do it in a more understandable way.

Gameplay
Paramania is an immersive puzzle solving game featuring an adventure in a Pyramid. The pyramid has a theme of light so basically all our puzzles are built around using the features of light: lighting up environment, reflection, color blending, etc. All interactions can be done using a VIVE controller.
There is a guide in the game (which is me) who leads players through the first scene, introducing the backstory and informing players to use the torch. After entering the pyramid the players will have to solve the puzzles on their own.
Design Elements
flow experience
At the early stage the game merely consists of multiple puzzles with little transition between each other, which for most players could be tedious. I then suggested that we have pre-scripted animations between two puzzles so the experience will have its relaxed and intense part.
Also, we want our game to have a epic and happy ending where players can actually see how the evil Pharoah's resurrection is ruined. So I make a pre-scripted clip for the ending:

unified theme
At first we have many ideas about puzzles and they all seem to work in some way. But after several we find that have just one unified theme for all puzzles will make it clearer for players and easier to control the difficulty for designers. We decided on "light" as our core element as it is COOL and uses CAVE environment in a smart way.
Design Process
think of hardware
We are putting players into a very special game environment where they are surrounded by three large screens so they are like ‘inside’ the game world. This is an excellent chance for us to make a fully immersive game so we start the exploration finding concept for a Egyptian pyramid where adventurers go and find treasures by solving puzzles.
idea iteration
We begin with thinking some possible interesting mechanism that could utilize the specialty of CAVE, they are: light reflection and refraction, matching patterns, triggering with flashlight, and drawing. Our first playable prototype was a mixture of different kinds of puzzles but later we all agreed that "light reflection" is the most suitable mechanism.
Post-Mortem
playtests!
We have some really nice puzzles and our environment really has Egyptian feeling, but that doesn’t work very well if the experience is way too long. In the video where naive guests play, they actually need 9 minutes to go through the game, which is much beyond the expected 6 minutes. It turns out we still need more playtests as new players may need more time to understand mechanics and enjoy the story.
