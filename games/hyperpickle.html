Project HyperPickle
HyperPickle is a client project built for picoCTF (Capture The Flag) 2021, a two-week online cybersecurity competition. During the fall 2020 semester, the team has built a game that's oriented around delivering a fun, engaging experience to attract people to join the competition - Adruppian Odyssey.

Background and Challenges
PicoCTF is an annual two-week-long cybersecurity online competition held by the CMU CyLab. The competition has been popular among middle school to college students across the globe, and the collaboration between the ETC and CyLab has been successfully running for years with a game built each year for the competition.

The games were designed primarily for middle school and high school students with little to intermediate knowledge in cybersecurity area. The lack of knowledge required necessarily to answer picoCTF challenges used to be a major setback when they were progressing the games that were dependent on challenges.

To address the solution, I present Adruppian Odyssey, a resource management game with idle elements that features the ability to exchange resources and make friends with inhabitants while exploring the interesting, diverse Adruppy System.
I had two roles. As co-producer:
- managed weekly sprints, communicating with our clients and the faculty;
- created visuals for branding materials;
As game designer:
- worked with the team to implement all game systems;
- orchestrated playtestings;
- improved user experience.
- summarized game design documents.
Pre-production
The team has spent the first four weeks in the semester understanding the project and getting prepared for full-production.
Researching
understanding the goal
The salient point of working on a delivery project is to know the target audience and understand the client’s requirements, so as to set goals accordingly. During the communication, we got to know that the majority of contestants who try the game will be middle to high school students with little to intermediate cybersecurity knowledge, and our client wanted the following: to engage them in the game, encourage them to answer different challenges, and entertain them with fun, positive content. We will not be, however, making an education game, or creating the challenges ourselves.
design challenges
The past picoCTF projects are great learning resources. The team all played the 2019 game which was the only one available.
Everything was fun till we were blocked by some of the harder challenges without answering which, the game wouldn’t progress. Being blocked by a single challenge and not able to experience the full story can be somewhat frustrating. Adding to the conversations with our clients, all design challenges we faced included:
- challenges being frustrating obstacles, not the motivation;
- unwillingness to jump out of comfort zone and try challenges in a different genre;
- to satisfy different tastes, especially female contestants who used to be the minority.
game psychology study
In the meantime, I was also studying several game genres and looked into the psychological effects these games possess that keep players engaged for a relatively long time (game study documentation).
With the problems defined and fundamental goal clarified, the team at the point headed to the game concept pitching phase smoothly.
Game Pitches
brainstorming!
I helped the team facilitate the brainstorming process by focusing on the game challenges mentioned above, with the notion that knowing clearly what we would be doing in mind helps with the efficiency: we kept evaluating every idea before speaking them out!
The team pitched dozens of rough sketches which could be further categorized into a few genre: collection games, linear storytelling with light puzzle elements, walk simulator, and even deck-building games.
I suggested we looked at our pitches in a critical way: for this project it's really not about how novel the gameplay is, but if the gameplay meets the requirements and if it's doable in a semester.
narrowing down
Having absorbed design principles of existing games, I suggested that we could start off with solutions and build story and detailed game mechanics based on that, just like adding flesh to the skeleton.
At this stage we wanted to try different approaches that were showing some potential. Instead of doing paper prototyping, thanks to our programming resources, we quickly built several digital prototypes in Unity and started to check the "fun" factor. Communicating with our clients, and considering the team's skills, we decided to make our game less story-heavy, but instead to use game content's traits of strong progression to achieve our design goal.
We are exploring an innovative way to redefine the relationship between the game and the contest this year. Instead of letting the player’s progress in the contest determine the progress in the game, we are making a game that could stand on its own even when no CTF challenge is solved.
planning for production
By the end of fourth week into the semester, our vision of the game has been clear: we would be making a resource management game that uses light idle element to nudge players to try picoCTF challenges, and the motivation comes from progressions and advancing stories.
Production
System Design
As suggested by the order of this article, I used bottom-up design approach for this game, and the reason was pretty straight-forward: for this game we were more like solving a problem: how to motivate the players to engage in the contest? Bottom-up helps build a mechanism that would well justify our solutions.
core gameplay loop
economy
Creating a balanced, compelling economy for such a resource management game is practically the salient task for game designers. Making full use of picoCTF contest as a motivation in the game soon became my solution for the problem: the tokens obtained from answering challenges would be functioning as a mobile game premium currency, that can be used to help play the game more efficiently (so players will want it), but not as a block impeding the normal game process.
MDA Analysis
When designing the game systems, I used the MDA framework which helped me clarify the interconnections of systems and evaluate their efficacy in the later stage.
progression system
Another thing we've been communicating with our clients was keeping players engaged during an as long as possible time period. The solution we came up with was the progression system. It's a big system with many components: basically, discovering new planets and upgrading the ship are all part of this system. What we did originally this year was the cosmetic items, namely the ship skins that player could unlock by obtaining pico challenge points. I designed these ship skin icons.
Story
I created the game's story using the traditional "5W1H" framework. I paid special attention to its complexity and understandability to suit middle and high school students.
Visual Design
As the team didn't have a dedicated UI designer onboard, I took the responsibility of user experience and worked closely with the programmer and artists on the team to implement visual elements in the game.
visuals vs. text
Our game has a complex resource system. Instead of using texts everywhere, I suggested using icons for the recourses to keep the interface clean and organized. Also to enhance the visual unity, we replaced resource name texts with icons in most systems' UI.

There's trade-offs too, of course, that it's hard for new players to recognize and remember those icons. To get around the problem, I suggested using mouse hover text to show the resource's name as it's pretty natural for people to hover their mouse over something they don't understand.
icons
Since we didn't have a dedicated UI designer, I also helped design some functional icons in the game with the primary design goal - be clear as possible.
Iteration & Hand-off
Playtesting
I made the playtesting plan and work with the team and our clients to do three rounds of playtesting with different purposes:
- Usability playtest: this smaller-scaled playtest was mainly to find game glitches, check if the player understands the goal, and if the UI in the game is working as expected.
- Game components playtest: in this larger playtest, we invited people outside and inside the ETC to try out the full game build, asking if they understand the main mechanics of the game, and if they feel the economy (thr most important part) was balanced.
- Challenge playtest: in this final playtest before handing off the game to our clients, we let many players try the polished build running on the website, and use real 2019 CTF challenges, as if in the real contest, to determine if the game system successfully motivated the players to try challenges while they are in the travel.
All three playtests proved to be successful in terms of massive feedback and suggestions that truly played a big part in our efforts to improve the game.
Enhancing User Experience
tutorials
We realized the importance of adding tutorials in the game after the first round of playtesting. I tried multiple approaches to do this, including:
- A compulsory go-through of game systems in the onboarding section;
- Pure text tutorial, also during onboarding;
- Step-to-step how-to tutorial images, with a question mark indicating in the main game UI that indicates its helpfulness.

With playtesters' and clients' feedback, we decided to use the third solution, because it's less forceful and saves space for story in the onboarding section, which would be an important plus for our middle to high school players.
While designing the tutorial images, I also paid attention to its readability and color theme. Here are two tutorial images used in the game. The semi-transparent black background covers the most UI, only exposing the part the help text is explaining.
tuning balance
During the third playtest we also asked directly in the survey their feeling about the game balance, and from the results we did hear some voice asking for buffing the pico tokens. In order to address the solution, we made traveling times between planets longer, but also buffed the token’s travel time reduction, to better encourage players to use tokens, and ultimately, to try pico challenges. In addition, we lowered the score threshold for contestants to get ship skins, according to the average score contestants obtained in 2019.
Reusable Framework
Another technical advancement made with our client this year is that we were developing a reusable framework for future picoCTF games. The code for answering CTF challenges is separated from the companion game code, and is integrated with the picoCTF’s frontend web page. In this way, future game teams can focus on game development, without worrying about handling CTF challenges in game.
configuration files
I worked with programmers to successfully create a configuration file that allows our client to modify some of our game's parameters when they have real challenges implemented.
Post-Mortem
The game will launch together with the main picoCTF competition scheduled in March 2021, with a projected number of dozens of thousands of contestants. I am truly glad to have had such a great chance of working with talented fellow students on such a great project, and we can’t wait to see how our game is going to influence such a big number of people!
What Went Well
Overall, given the difficulty of delivering a polished robust game product, the team performed admirably in getting tasks done in as quick a manner as we possibly could.

Product-wise speaking, our efforts in exploring and pivoting in the high-level game design from the previous games were unanimously acknowledged by both the faculty and our clients. The breakthrough can also be seen in an art style that no previous ETC teams has gone for, and the massive and well-organized in game systems that did the same job successfully: to engage players in a space journey, and try answering picoCTF challenges.

In terms of the production, the team showed off the spirit of unity working closely together despite the physical separation from each other. Everyone on the team really got the chance to shine in the roles they wanted to play instead of the ones they were forced to fill, and contributed to the ultimate success by an equal share.
What Could've Gone Better
The team had anticipated the difficulty of the remote working situation, however, to be honest, we probably could have been more communicative especially during the first month of the project when the team was doing fast idea iterations and cross-disciplinary communication was salient. To our relief, the team was able to address that point after the faculty meeting after Quarters, and had much smoother communication ever since.
