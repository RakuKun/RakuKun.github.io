"Meta-game" in Inside English Chinese
 First released in 2016, Inside is an incredible side-scrolling game that explores further and deeper in almost every aspect than developer’s previous critically claimed Limbo. What interests me most, above its marvelous graphics and intriguing story, is designer’s skill of combining levels and meta- game element for the unique art of storytelling.

 In so-called “hidden ending” the boy in the game pulls the plug of mind controlling device and felt to uncontrollable, which is often explained that the character gets out of player’s control as he manages to cut the relationship inside and outside the game (of course this is only one of the possibilities). This kind of gameplay is generally referred to “meta-game”, a concept that went heated in recent years. And since this word came to people’s eyes, it has been a novel, even selling point of a game. The interesting point of meta-game is the feeling of breaking the routine: the story may not go as you think it should be – something in the game may try to break the connection of storyline; the same applies to the character, the game objects and scenes. This skill is efficient as it solves the confliction between player interaction and storytelling. However, when more and more games use similar method, the meta-games seem to be less appealing as players begin to get familiar with the routine and even predict what will happen next. This shows the fact that “meta-game” is not beyond game: it is only a method that adds to the variety of game genres.

 What Inside does that excels some others, however, is the story and gameplay bound to it making Inside more of a “meta-game”. In the game there is no conversation, no narration, no explicit cultural symbols. Even that “hidden” ending does not tell anything related to the world setting. So how do we players make a “meta-game” guessing? Inside makes its “hidden” ending surprising but relatively understandable: it is based on the fact that players have all gone through the “normal” ending and the game puts forward merely an alternative option for the players. The two endings are not in confliction but explain each other respectively. We consider the “hidden” ending to have meta element as it seems to indicate the relationship between player and game, and we guess this relationship exists because the scenes, the game objects support our reasoning. We tend to think the character has cut the connection between him and player because in the game, many walking-deads are also under the charge of mind controlling devices – the reasoning is within game context. In a word, the meta element is the reasoning result of our gameplay experience, not the condition. The “meta” element Inside contains appears to be a rather inner part of its serious topic. The designers add difficulties for finding the “hidden” ending so we hardly discover the fact that we manipulate the life and death of the little boy. That is, in my opinion, more successful than using meta element to add fun or novelty to the game.

 What is more interesting, though, is the fact that the condition of reaching the hidden ending is so challenging that few people (at least no one around me!) are able to achieve without googling for a walkthrough. Players seek help merely for the hidden performance, for some experience they do not have the ability to get on their own. Does this further expand the definition of a meta-game: to influence the player help complete the game? I do not know, because by this definition it seems the range meta-game can cover is too broad. Inside is an interesting game because it provides with various aspect for players to talk about, and “meta” is only one of them which shows the possibility the designers can use to help tell a better story and form a thicker repressed atmosphere.
