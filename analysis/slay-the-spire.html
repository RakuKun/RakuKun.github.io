Slay the Spire: Engagement and Balance (ongoing)
Slay the Spire is a roguelike video game developed by American studio MegaCrit. The game was officially released in January 2019. This article will focus on the game's way to keep players engaged and how the game is balanced.
time spring 2020
Design the Uncommon
Slay the Spire is a roguelike, deck-building game. Just by the genre it is far from being so engaging and fun to play, absolutely there is some magic in game design that makes the common things uncommon.
roguelike
Though today the definition of roguelike is quickly changing as new roguelikes arise and old ones uncategorized, the long-time popular game genre has some elements unchanged. procedurally generated levels
Slay the Spire uses randomly generated level to dramatically increase replayability since every one is unique.
But is it really "random"? The answer is "NO". I would rather name it's semi-randomly creating levels. The choice is right since making levels should be designer's work, not computer's. So far as I have noticed, the game tries to keep a balance in different routes so you won't meet elites in a row and you can hardly find two campfires with nothing in between. And there are three certain things in the level:
The beginning of each stage is always a counter against normal enemies.
On about half way through each stage you will always run into a chest room.
Before fighting the boss of each stage (which is random) you have a chance of visiting a campfire.
The first one just sounds reasonable. The last two things really help decrease the chaos and let players take a precious rest when they really feel need to.
permadeath
I don't think permadeath is should be a must in roguelike games, in the modern context. The concept Roguelike started late '80s where games were considered a male sport and procedurally generated maps and permadeath were invented as effective ways to deal with the limitation in memory capability.
Slay the Spire also has permadeath, however, on the other hand, it does offer ways (seemingly consciously) for players to cope with failure in the game, by saving and loading. By replaying the combat the player got a chance to make up their mistakes and calculate their cards again. It is even not a glitch since the feature is too obviously to be considered as a program bug.
turn-based
Turn-based gameplay is another optional feature of roguelikes. The good thing for any turn-based games is that at least it is friendly for all players regardless of their "reflection speed" or "fighting instinct", so it is potentially designed for all ages.
