Slay the Spire: Engagement and Balance
Slay the Spire is a roguelike video game developed by American studio MegaCrit, officially released in January 2019. This article will try analyzing game's approach to keep a high engagement whilst balanced at the same time.
time spring 2020
Creating the Uncommon
Slay the Spire is a roguelike, deck-building game. According to an article on Gamasutra, roguelike games benefit from "compartmentalizing their core gameplay loop while still providing long-term value" so many of them stands out in today's video game environment where "customers don't have the time to find the fun of a title". Slay the Spire has a great pick-up-and-play feel, given that players know they will either win or lose usually in less an hour.
So the question is, what exactly makes each run feel so engaging?
roguelike
Though today the definition of roguelike is quickly changing as new roguelikes arise and old ones uncategorized, the long-time popular game genre has some elements unchanged.
procedurally generated levels
Slay the Spire uses randomly generated level to dramatically increase its replayability. In a relatively simple way, each run is slightly different from any previous one, and players meet different enemies and bosses, get different cards and relics, which eventually makes players' combat choice and overall strategy. Randomness is a great help when designers want to build an entertainment that can then be repeated with variance and new challenges again and again.

The magic of having randomness is that it fits the essence of "game" and the nature of human desire for an unknown adventure. Games are an entertainment both challenging and rewarding. In Slay the spire players face different challenging situation as they start a run and go deeper, but the game keeps rewarding them with more powerful cards and relics, which forms an ideal engagement curve.
permadeath
I don't think permadeath should be a must in roguelike games, especially in the modern context. The concept Roguelike started late '80s where games were considered an exclusive sport for male and that procedurally generated maps and permadeath were invented as effective ways to deal with the limitation in computer's memory capability. Today, almost every home PC can run a card game like Slay the Spire, but the game still uses permadeath to remind players of the importance of their choices so as to put some nervosity on each move. And when the players start to think of their actions seriously, they are been already dragged into the world and keep the high commitment.

The game, however, on the other hand, has left ways (seemingly on purpose) for players to cope with permadeath in the game, by saving and loading. By replaying the combat the player got a chance to make up their mistakes and calculate their cards again. It is a glitch that everyone uses to save time and play better.
turn-based
Turn-based gameplay is another optional feature of roguelikes. The good thing for any turn-based games is that at least it is friendly for all players regardless of their "reflection speed" or "fighting instinct", so it is potentially designed for all ages.
deck-building
Deck building is another thing that makes Slay the Spire engaging and addicting. The ultimate reason for this, in my opinion, is the same motivation for playing an RPG - the feeling of growth. Starting with basic cards to eventually beating the final boss, it is deck that makes up the majority of visible growth in the game.
playing with randomness
Players have the chance to choose from three random cards of your color after every battle. Again, all three random cards may be just useless, but players can choose skip the cards. The designers want players on different level all feel good about their choices, so giving optional choices is still important. People all like to see what the system offers - it always feels great to gamble without concerning losing money, right?
And it even feels better when system drops upgraded cards, which gives a feeling that "whatever I pick I hit pay dirt".
The same design philosophy applies to the events, too. Events happening in ? rooms share the high risk - high reward trait, and the ability to accept the offer or not is granted to the players: new players may skip the risk (and reward) while experienced players could play in a more exciting pace. Gambling is mankind's nature - Slay the Spire manipulates that.
the changing strategy
Slay the Spire, like many other games, has short goals and long-term goals. Finding a best strategy to beat the next enemy fight is the short goal, and beating a run is the long goal. This brings the discussion about the altering strategy. In early phases picking up a card is very worth considering since they are almost all better than basic cards, but in late phases having a new card is no longer that tempting once their deck is stable enough where one new card could break the synergy and the potential of bad draws.

The dynamic is in fact happening in every choice in Slay the Spire, which explains why late games in Slay the Spire bring more intensity as players reach their long-term goal.
Fighting the Chaos
Comes along the pure randomness the pure chaos. Players' expectation would be ruined as the game is totally out of their control. Players would easily lose track of what they are doing in the game as what they did will prove not applicable for later combats. The solution to this, would be balancing the uncertainty brought by the roguelike elements while not damaging them meanwhile.
strategy
levels
Apparently, levels are not created by a single function - CreateRandomLevels(). I would name it semi-random levels since creating levels should be designer's work where algorithms are used to repeat their design choices. So far as I have noticed, the game tries to keep a balance in different routes so you won't meet elites in a row and you can hardly find two campfires with nothing in between. And there are three certain things in the level:
- The beginning of each stage is always an encounter against normal enemies.
- On about half way through each stage you will always run into a chest room.
- Before fighting the boss of each stage (which is random) you will always be visiting a campfire.

The first one just sounds reasonable. The last two things really help decrease the chaos and let players take a precious rest when they really feel need to.
intent system
Intent system is probably the most important feature developers has put in the game so far (actually from their talks this is their proudest thing). In Slay the Spire's early version, players could not see what enemies' next move is, and the result was very frustrating since the computer instead of players controls the result of each combat.
Luckily the designers created the enemies' intent to balance the uncertainty.
resources
There are by category three kinds of first-level resources in the game that adds on to the real combat capability. Money is another very useful resource, but it is a second-level resource that needs to be converted into first-level resources to make it really work.

Since each combat is the minimum compartment of a run, most resources are used during the combat. There are, on the other hand, some resources that could affect different parts of the game.
cards
Cards are permanent resources that come into effect every combat and serve as the most fundamental and important resources in the game. As mentioned, players can choose to pick one from three cards to their deck after each battle. The game tries to keep the balance between the deck and enemies at a dynamic pace.

Cards' cost and effect in Slay the Spire is a very interesting topic. There is believed to be a standard model for basic cards - 1 energy for 6 attack or 5 defense. If any card fails to exceed this cost-effect ratio, it would generally be considered not useful.
It is a clear model. But in most cases it is hard to determine the effect without putting it under an actual environment. For instance, 1 energy to draw 3 (4) cards is extremely powerful in late games where you have high energy and many cards whose synergy can produce much more than one energy, but you probably don't want to have lots of this card in the first Phase since your deck is far from stable and powerful. Good thing is, there are some easier ways to tweak the balance (roughly).

There are three basic attributes in a card: cost, rarity, and effect. Generally cards with high rarity and cost have better effect. There is an inherent balanced between the two, and on the crossroad is the trigger limitation. With this in mind, designers could avoid overtuning or undertuning cards in a large scale.
relics
Relics are permanent resources that are triggered passively to influence the combat, or to give other one-time benefits. Roguelikes likes to give players chances to gain equipments because in most cases, they give players general buff so they can cope with everchanging and increasingly difficult levels. Relics are general so however players' deck is composed, they are possibly effective, and the effect in fact multiples with more relics, not adds up.

Slay the Spire wants more out of that. There are drops character-only relics that specifically powers up a certain character's ability, and some seemingly strange ones that are powerful in odd situations.
These relics are very contradictory - they can enable a deck, but they are useless as well if picked up in late game where the deck doesn't need the features any longer.
potions
Potions are consumable resources that are used in the combat. The designers want the potions to be more powerful in combats, so they actually created a few relics that centers the using of potions.
The relic-potion combo may be the most easy-to-understand build in the game, but the relics are not so easy to find - especially when you have already targeted at one build. Many new players, including me, want a build that is strong, stable, and easy to get at the same time. but the game teaches us the lesson: everything is changing fast, and adjusting the strategy according to the cards and relics received is a must-learn. It is just like our life.
data
So how to balance such a number of cards? Designers can let players help with the work. There is another title on Gamasutra about using the player data to balance their game.
why data
Playtesting have been among the highest priority in modern game development since games are less like personal expressions but more collective commercial products, where catering to players' expectation becomes most significant thing. And modern games are enormous - systems, programming, animations, sounds... The easiest way to find bugs is just to let someone play the game.

This is especially true in Slay the Spire, the game with only two full-time developers. And there are so many cards with different abilities. Asking the players to play and treating them as playtesters by collecting their in-game data, is a smart move for designers.
metrics
According to the article, the two most important metrics, are "how often a player picks a card when given the choice, and how often a card appears in a winning deck." From a designer's perspective, every card in the game should be unique and function at least under some circumstances. But for players, cards have a usage hierarchy and that determines if the card is well designed or not: if no one chooses to pick up a card, then it is basically not a card in game at that point. If a card appears too often in the winning decks, it is probably overpowered which also breaks the balance.
beyond balance
Overall, the game should be fun. The fact that the game is a single-player roguelike makes balancing a lot easier than for most competitive card games. Players are not competing against each other, so decks do not need to be equally powerful, and the designers could make changes for entertainment value rather than in the name of pure balance. The randomness and deck-building offers major source of fun, while balancing the cards ensures the fun is controlled in a reasonable range. Chaos and order come dancing together.
