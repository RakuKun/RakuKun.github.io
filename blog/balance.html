Aspects of Game Balance
Balancing a game means lots of things. In an intuitive way it's about numbers, but that's only the micro side of balancing. The macro side - game flow - also should always be at a significant place in the process of game making.
time spring 2020
Macro: A Balanced Game Flow
The game flow I recently played a little decent game: First Cut on itch.io, and I would like to use this game as the analysis object. It is not perfectly balanced, which makes the analysis more meaningful and targeted.
Setup
A balanced setup means the balanced start and goal, and in skill-based games, more precisely, the start and goal of each side should be the same. Both sides spawn with the same ability on symmetrical sides on the stage. Their goal is also the same: to one-shot each other. The only difference they can make to the characters is the characters' appearance but since the change is only decorational and not confusing, it is far from breaking the visual balance. So I could say its setup is well balanced.
Dynamic
A balanced dynamic means that in one roundthe passive side still has the chance to strike back, and in each separate round players compete under the same condition. In this game I call the attacking side the aggressive side and the defending side the passive. There is an important rule that helps the passive side strike back: If you press attack immediately after blocking you will perform a counter attack. This makes counter-attack strategy possible and rewarding.

The game won’t nerf the previous winner or buff the loser, every new round starts the same with previous ones. This ensures that if two players are on the same skill level, after many rounds their chance of winning should be near 50-50. I was playing this game with my classmate Aiden and we’ve played for around 300 rounds and our winrate is almost equal. We struggled to understand how every line of rule meant in the game, and as we proceeded to figure that out, we just found more fun in the mechanism.
Choices
Balanced choices mean that all choices given are equally useful and effective. In general, there are several movesets with some slightly different attributes. There are three types of attack exactly as the rules explained. Since all attacks are one-shot, the difference between them is range and speed.

As Aiden and I tested out, in fact the most effective defending style in game is still blocking since it does not require any additional input and allows counter-attack right after. We found sidestep less useful because it disallows dodging while executing the action, and moved the character a short distance which may confuse players because their relative positions may flip (after sidestepping A may goes behind B).
Interaction
Balanced interaction means the interaction between human and computer is fair among hardware. The game allows its multiplayer mode to be played by 1 keyboard and 1 gamepad, and that is probably the most unbalanced element in this game due to the nature of different hardware. We all know that many games have strict constraints with regard to cross-platform playing because either mouse-keyboard or gamepad may have strong hardware advantage over the others, especially in shooting games (CoD, Battlefield, Apex, etc.) and fighting games. In this game, we did feel that gamepad and mouse-keyboard differ in the game feel, especially when we were trying to direction and dodging.
Micro: A Balanced Model
From a micro perspective the word "balance" means the mathematical model for each player is expected to be equal.
To Avoid Blurriness
When I introspects the reasons for encountering design hardship and failures, for many times I was designing only with intuitive thoughts instead of the analysis of the real problem. Intuitive thoughts come from the preexisting game experience, and appear like "if I am designing this I would use the way XXX uses since that feels great". A symbolic example: when people complain a hero in a MOBA is overpowered, they are talking their own match experience, while for different ranks a same hero may perform much differently.

Intuitive thoughts feels plausible and are easy to think of, but the downside is that they often come blurred and off the track. The blurriness comes from our tricky memory that does not always serve 100% accurate. For game amateurs that may be sufficient, but dangerous especially for game designers who should instead use their professional knowledge to analyze and solve the problem. Game designers have rich experience in games which may deceive them as a side effect.

Balancing a game, as a job of game designers', should always stick to the common sense of designing: to define a problem, to disintegrate its parts, to do research, and to come up with multiple ways from which designers choose the most suitable one. It is a highly rational, logical process with careful concerns over any possibility of deteriorating the situation. That highlights the significance of having a balanced mathematical model for a game.
Establishing Models
First to clarify: games don't necessarily originates from a math model. There are basically two types of game design method: Bottom-up and Top-down. While bottom-up approach pieces together of mechanics to give rise to a more complex system, top-down approach has a theme/idea formed first and breaks it down to gain insight into details. But every game will eventually come to a stage where the process of establishing a math model should occur.

Even the simplest games have some rules for which the designers and programmers set up a series of variables to handle the input and output. For instance, Flappy Bird where all players can do is touching the screen within the correct time period.

To start off, as the game designer, we need to figure out what parameter is ultimately impacting the game experience (which is usually a dependent variable):
- The "grace" time during which the fly input is successful.
And there are the independent variables that we have actually control:
- The bird's flying speed.
- How fast the bird drops and how far can bird jump upwards.
- The distance between pillars that allows the bird to fly through.
- The distance between two stops.
With these parameters set up, and say we start right between the first pair of pillars, we can easily know the time it takes to travel to the next challenge:
- TravelTime = Distance / FlySpeed;
And if we assume the bird falls at a constant speed, we can know how far it falls traveling:
- FallDistance = FallSpeed * TravelTime;
As we observe in the game, usually the bird needs to flap wings multiple times in between each level in order not to fall, so we also have: - FlyDistance = FlapDistance * FlapTimes.
Now we come to the final answer,
- GraceDistance = PillarDistance - (FallDistance - FlyDistance)
- GraceTimePeriod = GraceDistance / FlapDistance;

With our key variable being represented by independent variables the designers can start tweaking the value and add more variables should they like. One thing worth noting is that we start designing under the ideal situation to better expose the core design problem and add complexity step by step.
Complex Situation
In more complex circumstance, designers may spend more time setting up an appropriate model. Let's say we have an action game like Furi and we need to balance the boss's hitpoints. Generally the first thing is to lay out the component of combat:
- A hero who can do basic attack and perform skills with separate cooldowns. The hero also has some items that could boost up themselves.
- A boss the death of whom signals the victory of combat. The boss also has its own skills.
- A limited combat arena disallowing anybody to flee.
Then we determine key variables that will definitely influence the game experience:
- Hero's attack damage, skill damage and cooldowns.
And it's common sense that we don't want the characters to just one-shot the boss, so in long-term there will be the so-called DPS cycles that makes the boss feel "tanky":
Boss HP = (Sum of [((optimal DPS + item DPS) * Suboptimal)]) * Cycles.

The formula itself is seemingly easier to understand and has even fewer variables than the Flappy Bird's. But again we started off with least variables as possible, such as optimal DPS as we know in game it's impossible to follow the optimal pattern because boss also has skills and forces heroes to sacrifice some of DPS for survival. And in an action game there are a lot more choices every moment that designers should be able to predict. With this in mind, when we are designing boss's skills we can always refer to the formula above and see what impact it could bring to the existing variables, and that process can be very tricky as all variables are dependent on others and changing one value could possibly break the experience.
