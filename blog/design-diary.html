Game Design Diary
This design diary is to record game design takeaways collected via courses, playing games, and thinking.
time spring 2020 -
What is a Game?
  It is very hard to give a precise definition of game because it is changing so fast with everyday new type of games coming out. Stone Librande has listed several key elements in his definition: start, goal, obstacles and rules, which really makes sense in an objective way.
For me, game should contain one more element - "fun". Admittedly, having fun or not is very subjective since everyone has their own judgement, yet to try to get everyone feel interested and happy should always be the highest goal in a designer's perspective purely.
What is a Good Game?
  This question is even harder than the previous one! I think "good" is both an objective and subjective word. It is objective so game retailers such as Steam have a score system that collects and shows players' reviews. It is subjective so we have several measurements by which we can roughly evaluate if a game is worth spending money and time, and luckily by looking at game elements we can have a way to define each measurement.
  Rules. Good games deliver their rules in a clear, understandable way, regardless if 8-year or 80-year is playing. In addition, good rules should be using the minium rules to weave the maximum possibility. Rules not only influence how the game runs, but also how players think and make decisions. So providing players a bunch of choices is good, and it would be better if the rules can make the choices generate themselves.
  Goals. Good games don't always set a goal for players. For instance, Minecraft (which I think is a good game) don't actually have systematic goals, instead different kinds of players have their own things to do: architects build fabulous mansions, adventurers travel around the world and kill monsters, community players do meaningless things together just to enjoy the group time. On the other hand, for those with a goal, the goal, and the player's relationship to it, is crystal clear. Dramatic tension increases as the player gets closer to the goal.
  Narrative & Interaction. Again not all good games even have a story, especially true when we look back to the '80s, games like Pac-Man told the backstory on the package rather than in-game clips. However, good interaction can really make up the missing narrative. Players enter the game world and by controlling the game characters, they forget about the real world. Nowadays, with the help of advancing hardware and software, games are enabled to tell immersive stories and drag the players into the created world, and the best would be that the interaction pattern fits the narrative.
What is a Good Story?
I like RPG and I won't invest time in an RPG that fails to tell a good story since I think story is so crucial as an element to make the game logical and meaningful. Games, as other forms of art do, shares the same principle for writing a story.
For me the most important thing of a good story is conflict. It is two opposing forces collider with one another. Conflict could make the dramatic moment really strong and keep the game at a high level of tension.
Games have naturally multiple ways to tell stories: visual, sound, acting, and even item's description. The possibility is open, the thing is to find an efficient and effective way to do the narrative.
