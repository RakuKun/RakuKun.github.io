Hanzo in HotS and MOBA Hero Design
In this article I will try to discuss why I think Hanzo is a well-designed hero in Heroes of the Storm...
...And more generally, what is a good hero design in MOBA games.
time winter 2020
Hanzo in Heroes of the Storm
"One shot, one kill."

I liked playing Hanzo a lot when I was a (really!) active Heroes of the Storm (HotS) player who got introduced to the game when playing him in Overwatch. Despite that I played Hanzo very badly in Overwatch mostly because of my bad FPS aiming skill, miraculously I got used to Hanzo's playstyle in HotS relatively quickly, which eventually pushed me to be a well-rounded ranged assassin player in HotS.

And as a game designer I think that Hanzo is a really well-designed hero as in HotS. His design fits the word "elegant". In the following paragraphs I will be trying to use my personally-adjusted MDA Framework to list out the supporting points, as well as more generally, what are the major metrics for evaluating a MOBA hero's design from a designer's perspective.
Mechanics
On the mechanic level, a hero's design can be broken down to skills and stats, as these are the fundamental attributes of the hero that have a direct impact on how players will play them. I will start with Hanzo's skillset.
In Heroes of the Storm, Hanzo is a Ranged Assassin. The Ranged Assassin role, once is described in HotS as following: Ranged Assassin heroes are fragile damage dealers that are able to attack their enemies from a safer distance. And Hanzo specifically, was described in a single sentence as: "A ranged Assassin who specializes in scouting and taking out enemies from a distance."

Hanzo's design perfectly fits both of the descriptions. His Q, W, and two ultimates all can deal significant amount of damage from a distance, and his Basic Attack (BA) deals second highest damage among HotS's heroes' BAs. His E skill provides a temporary zone of vision for the team. Long range attacks also mean a great poke ability.

On the other hand, Hanzo is a fragile hero. his basic HP is low and falls in the typical Ranged Assassin's range but he still gets a long-cooldown escape skill that can rid him out of danger sometimes. He also gets no self-sustain ability and thus relies heavily on healers for HP. Having some weakness in a hero is absolutely important because only that gives the chance for opponents to find a way to counteract his advantages.

The second thing designers can use to evaluate a hero's balance design are pick rate and win rate, among casual/low rank/high rank (professional) matches. It's dangerous to say a hero's too strong or weak while not putting them in a specific environment. Hanzo was a hero that's universally picked as the choice for team's damage dealer especially in higher ranked matches for his good performance with skilled players. His win rate is not that high though, on the other hand, as several batches had already made him a pretty balanced hero. Hanzo's win rate is lower when we look at it in every match in all tiers, which again proves that Hanzo is not an easy hero - to play him well you need to practice his skills a lot.
On top of stats, "balance" in MOBA games in terms of win rates, as I always believe, are only two situations. The first are the so-called newbie-friendly heroes. They are easy to learn, easy to master, have pretty balanced win rates among different tiers. Pro players may also use them in competitive games for their trustworthy consistency. The second is easy-to-learn-hard-to-master heroes like Hanzo. They are definitely challenging for new players, but have the potential to outperform in terms of win rate when used by skillful players. This kind of heroes are popular in competitive matches because pro players have rich experiences to compensate for the risk.
Dynamics
The dynamic level in this topic specifically, in my opinion, is how the hero's design fit into the game's other systems and therefore ultimately, leverage the fun factor. In HotS there are two aspects: Terrains and Talents.

Hanzo's W and Trait skill makes full use of HotS's Terrain system. Most heroes cannot see over, or go through terrain so it's a tactically important part of the battleground. Hanzo's W skill, Scatter Arrow can collide with terrain and structures, splitting into extra arrows. His Trait allows him to jump over terrain. Not only these skills is hard to use well, but more importantly, they leverage the player's awareness of Terrain, which will eventually deepen their understanding of the game's systems. A skilled Hanzo player understands terrain's geometry well and can use this extra knowing as their advantage against some other heroes that have skills that interact with the terrain. It's a virtuous circle. What I'd like to address here is that such design doesn't always mean developing a new system exclusively for the character; it's about how the character "blend in" the game environment.
Hanzo's Talent choices are incredibly diverse and fun to learn. In short, Talents in Heroes of the Storm are unique perks that players choose to unlock for their heroes at some certain levels in game.
Hanzo's Talents allow three totally different playstyles. The Q build makes Hanzo a true poker and also grants him good waveclear ability. The W build is the hardest among three builds, but enables Hanzo to be the main carry on the team thanks to its cooldown reduction and consistent damage output. The Basic Attack build takes Talents that dramatically improve Hanzo's sustained damage. This build has its own risk as Hanzo will lose the Talent's bonus attack speed upon dying.
Hanzo's Heroic Abilities (ults), allows players to make Hanzo a pure damage machine or have some control ability. These Talents altogether makes Hanzo really an interesting hero to play, and to learn. It's always a challenging task for a MOBA game to keep player's freshness even when they've already played for 2000 hours - while there are many heroes in a MOBA game, designing a brand new hero is always a tough task for the developers. Therefore, making a hero' learning process rewarding and enjoyable is a good solution to work around the problem. Hanzo's design ensures that it will take some time to master this hero - perhaps hundred of matches, but learning to play him is fun and exciting, because again, you're in fact learning to play in three drastically different Ranged Assassin playstyles!
Aesthetics
In the MDA Framework, Aesthetics are the emotional level that directly influence the player. Hanzo is originally an Overwatch character before he went into Heroes of the Storm, and "Preciseness" is the word that best described him. All(!) of his skills in HotS are skillshots (abilities that fire a projectile from the source in the chosen direction), making Hanzo a very easy-to-learn-hard-to-master hero: a bad player might miss every shot except for Basic Attacks, while an experienced player can make full use of each skill and inflict damage on target heroes. His W skill, Scatter Arrow is a juxtaposition of the identically named skill in Overwatch (but was later removed) that uses the Terrain as its core mechanism. In short, playing Hanzo in HotS feels much like playing him in Overwatch, even though the two games are mechanically so different. Elegantly lethal and precise. It's the design work that has helped reach such a success.>
In addition to that, Hanzo's gameplay in HotS also involves lots of interesting player-player interactions. All of his level 1 Talents are quests of which the conditions are to use the skill (or BA) to hit enemy heroes a certain times. To me this is far more interesting than "kill 100 minions to power up the skill" which can be done more mindlessly. To do Hanzo's missions the player need to be more aggressive in laning, which could result in exposing the hero to a dangerous situation. Hanzo's skills like E skill and Dragon's Arrow have big tactical usefulness in terms of gaining sight and stunning enemies, thus making a perfect team combo possible.

Conclusion
I think Hanzo in Heroes of the Storm is the best result of transplanting a hero from another universe, and is successfully designed in almost every aspect. In this article I used the MDA Framework to do the analysis, and the following metrics, in my opinion, proves to be useful to evaluate the design of a character in MOBA games:
Mechanics:
- Does the character's design, working together, fit their described role?
- Are the character's stats/numbers well balanced, not too strong or too weak?
Dynamics:
- Does the character's abilities come in harmony with the game's other systems?
- Is it fun to learn to play the character?
- Do players have diverse choices on how they can develop the character?
Aesthetics:
- Does the design fits the character's backstory and the settings of the game?
- Does the design encourage interesting player-player interaction?
