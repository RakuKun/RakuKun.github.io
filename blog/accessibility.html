Accessibility - Game For All
Games should always be an opportunity to experience something beyond everyday life for all people. This article discuss some successful practices in designing an working accessibility system.
time fall 2020
For and From Accessibility
This article was written in September 2020, when the world was still suffering from the Covid-19 pandemic. When I was staying home working and playing video games, it occured to me some day that I needed game more than ever to escape the dullness, and I think my situation applies to many people in WFH status. On top of that, with the rapid revolution in game systems' complexity, graphics, and multiplayer features, the need for a working accessibility settings is of much prominence now. Making sure that players with different cultural background, different physical abilities are all able to enjoy the game is the fundamental spirit of equity for a game studio.

I have been playing Naughty Dog's games and I am glad to find they really put some effort into making accessible games. Here's the video about Accessibility in Uncharted 4. What's more, their accessibility system got polished and was almost reinvented in The Last of Us 2, making it probably the most accessible triple-A game so far. Here's their blog on Accessibility in TLOU 2.
Types of Accessibility
Building accessibility features for games could somehow refer to the study in human-computer interaction field since the object of interaction is kind of the same: an input hardware (mouse or controller) and several output hardware (monitor, speaker and yes, controller).

There are several basic catagories of accessibility assets:
- Vision accessibility
- Hearing accessibility
- Motion accessibility
- Culture accessibility
Keep the Fun Factor
What I want to stress is that building an accessibility system for game is more than doing that for a medical device or so. The designers should keep in mind that the accessibility should not be at odds with creative gameplay. With or without the accessibility feature the game still runs for everyone and creates the same illusion where people are put on a great journey that's mostly impossible in the real world. Because of the importance of keeping the fun factor, people with disabilities should be considered as a serious part in the playtesting.

It's also worth noting that a good accessibility system applies to all players, not only those with actual disabilities. Sometimes people want to use such function for nothing but simpler experience, sometimes they are not so good at certain controls - designers should let people know that such assistance is normal and really open to everyone.
Vision Accessibility
Vision accessibility includes color blindness, nearsightedness, and other functions for bringing a better vision.
Color Blindness
Most games work with colors (though in Ghost of Tsushima you can choose to play in black and white) and luckily many of them already have a solid system to assist people with color blindness. Some games do better in providing different even customizable settings for different types of color blindness: protanopia, deuteranopia, tritanopia and more. Here's an example from Battlefield V. (The color is actually customizable in RGB.)
However, most color blindness assist focuses on only the UI part, leaving a huge space for improvement in environmental and character design.
Nearsightedness
In fact, Nearsightedness happens more than people would figure, because games can make people nearsighted too! It sounds weird but for some people whose first language is not English and are playing the game with subtitles on like me, sometimes the font size is so small that I have to move closer to the monitor in order to read them. I feel like I have nearsightedness in those moments. Tweaking the UI still fixes most of the problem. But again, for some game where enemies attacks you from distance or where you need to stealth through the guards, it can really be a nightmare if you cannot see them clearly in the screen. And night environment is a natural obstacle for people to observe. There are multiple ways to help with the trouble (and some are being used already I'm sure):
- A binocular functions in some game to compensate the sight loss.
- When you are running or hiding, the game camera automatically zooms in a bit to help observe the environment.
Other Tricks
One very interesting visual aid I found is using high contrast color to actually help players not be distracted by environment.
Playing in such render mode makes me feel I'm hacking the game, not actually experience it normally - I fail to enjoy the beauty of the graphics, but it does help someone who's having trouble recognizing stuff in normal render mode.

It feels more like a game feature, but in many game levels that run completely in night scenes, it's not rare for the system to provide with a thermal vision/night vision, like Sam's iconic night vision goggles in Splinter Cells series.
These tricks are more like accessibility features because they do provide more information for the player - and that's not balanced for the ones that don't use them.
Hearing Accessibility
Hearing accessibility is crucial for people in need. In order to consider how to assist those players, we can first make a list of the possible sounds in the game, and design accessibility features accordingly:
- Dialogue: subtitle can be useful and implementable. The problem is that sometimes it does not offer enough information. Another way is to pin a location/item on the map so everybody can know without shouting out.
- Sound effects, like gunfire or special item. These sounds can actually be "shown" in the game if properly displayed by a sound indicator, such as bullet trail, or a shooting direction UI.
Another good way I find is to highlight the figure rims like The Last of Us. When used well it doesn't break the game flow.
- Environmental sound/music. From a storytelling perspective it's important to hear the grand composition, but from a gameplay standpoint, it plays little role in helping define where the enemy is, so normally games don't really visualize it, but there are several nice try.

As you probably find, one principle for designing a hearing accessibility feature is try to visualize the sound, as our vision system is in fact able to receive more information than we think.
Motion Accessibility
Motion accessibility, in my opinion, is something that should work for everyone. Everybody can have motion sickness sometimes. Everybody has their preferred input mode and presets that often differ from game to game, console to console.

When we talk about motion sickness, two camera effects are really to blame: camera shake and motion blur. Luckily most games allow players to switch on/off the function or tweak the strength. The most common motion settings available in most games is the option to switch left/right joystick, or to switch the left/right click on the mouse.

In VR games the problem of motion sickness becomes more salient. From many experiences building VR games, here's some of the takes I think could work:
- Slow down! A fun but true fact is that in VR world we feel things faster than they actually move. So it's okay if we really slow them down a little bit, because your brain is likely to magnify the speed anyway. When things move too fast for the brain to figure out what's happening, it starts a strike.
- Use a vignette mask. Vignette masks get rid of some information in the screen's corners, helping players focus on the center of the screen. The scientific theory behind this is when human eyes receive less information at a time, they are less likely to exhaust over time.
- Have something stay in the screen. This thing works as a "reference object" in the movement, and since its position is fixed in the screen, it creates an illusion that you are not moving that fast and things are not changing that quickly.
Culture Accessibility
It's not common but people from different culture may encounter something in the game that causes confusion, for example, bad subtitles.

Developers usually pay attention not to symbolize religion, or custom habit in a way that not truthfully reflect the real world. What they fail to acknowledge sometimes, is that when talking about a different culture, there are much more things that are not common sense: for western developers on a game based on Japanese history, not judging and designing people from a western moral standpoint is what it takes to success. Ghost of Tsushima did a good job creating a loyal, fearless ghost warrior from a story standpoint, and it's worth praising that they invited Japanese voice actors to record the dialogue. What is missing to me is that the mouth animation was still motion captured in English dialogue, so when I turned to Japanese voice, it kind of stuck out like a sore thumb.
