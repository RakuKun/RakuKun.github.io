On Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment
Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment is shaping how people play games in a way few have ever imagined: it’s changing how players perceive, evaluate, and even judge themselves in the game. However, they face some inherent challenges for which, as I argue in this article, higher transparency to users and the ability to be optional should be seriously considered.
time winter 2020
Part 1: A Lawsuit
Gamers know Electronic Arts (EA) well for its annual releases of EA Sports. However, in recent years things have changed. In November 2020, a lawsuit was filed against EA for its alleged use of the Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment system in three of its EA Sports franchises -- Madden NFL, FIFA, and NHL that can be traced back to 2017, the year when EA publicized a patent exactly called Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment [2]. The plaintiffs said that EA uses this technology to push players into purchasing more loot boxes in the form of Player Packs, saying that it effectively makes even high stat players not play as well as they should. Despite EA’s consistent denial of its use of DDA to manipulate the players actions on spending real dollars to buy in-game boost-up packs, it’s admittedly a fact that DDA is in a big reputation maelstrom now - and the lack of transparency and choices from player’s end, is at least partly to blame.

But before diving into the moral and efficacy problems, a clarification of what is Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment and how it works in a game would be helpful.

Most commonly used in electronic video games, Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment (DDA), is an AI-based system that allows the change of attributes and behaviors within the game in runtime, adaptively changing a game to make it easier or harder. It automatically collects the in-game user data and measures the player performance and changes the game difficulty to match the player’s skills, while the process of adaptation should not be clearly perceived by the players. Its work flow can be separated into three parts: The first step needed to implement a DDA system is an evaluation of the player’s performance which may include determining variables in the game, data collection, setting up reference points, and data analysis. Next the system uses models such as metrics, probabilistic methods, dynamic scripting to achieve the DDA. Finally, the system keeps analyzing the efficacy and adjusting till it reaches a “balanced” difficulty curve [3].
Part 2: The Curtain of Distrust
Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment (DDA) is not, at least academically, a new thing. Back in 2005, a paper by Hunicke has talked about the use of DDA and its effectiveness in boosting the player performance while maintaining the sense of agency and accomplishment [4]. In recent years, although it’s still a myth how many games have used or are using the DDA system, the discussion of DDA is getting noticeably more and more heated [5]. Despite the fact that Electronic Arts (EA) never officially acknowledged its use of DDA, in 2017 there’s a paper from researchers from EA detailing the use of a DDA framework targeting at maximizing a player’s engagement in a level-based game [6].

But why do developers make DDA perform undetected in the first place? Some argue that players will feel being “cheated” if the difficulty of the game is adjusted during or across play sessions, which harms its efficacy and ultimately user experience in the game, and it kind of became a common practice. But many people just don’t like being manipulated by “algorithms” rather than being defeated by a real human.

However, despite the proven effectiveness of such a system in real-world applications, there’s a gap between players and games that have to be filled: trust. Complaints on EA Sports games have shown that (regardless of whether DDA is actually being used) the trust factor can dramatically affect how players perceive the game’s system as helpful or evil, and eventually impact their user experience. A highly in-theory effective system will not perform well if the users don’t trust it - when a ill human-AI system is established, users tend to doubt every decision the system made for them, and possibly go the opposite way or even ditch the system. On the opposite end, the system’s benefits will be realized when the technology succeeds to foster appropriate user reliance.

One of the many ways proven by experiments that automated systems (and DDA) can use to obtain user’s trust is to build up the system with higher transparency to its users [7]. What is necessary to address here is, transparency is not equal to “disclosing everything” which almost all for-profit companies will shy away from. Among multiple ways to achieve higher transparency, a limited but appropriate disclosure of logic-based explanations for the recommendations was found effective and, in my opinion, feasible for video games. According to a paper on how the disclosure of DDA in the game will affect the player’s attributes, the full awareness of DDA proved to be a plus for low-performing players in competence but reduced their autonomy, which again certifies the fact that to what extent the use of DDA should be exposed [8].
Part 3: The Nature of Flow
As mentioned before, Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment is not a new thing but gains popularity in recent years. Its theoretical base can be traced back to the Flow theory in the late 90s [9]. Flow, as a psychological term, means an “optimal experience, where we feel a sense of exhilaration, a deep sense of enjoyment that is long cherished” as explained in the original article.
Games are a booming industry: it’s not rare for a game to sell to dozens of millions of people, who have, of course, varying levels of skills. For developers, a realistic problem is how to handle such a diverse player background. This is where the concept of Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment came into play, initially. For many years it’s believed that the flow experience in video games can be achieved through proper implementation of the DDA system, thanks to the system’s ability to observe, adapt, and influence player’s in-game performance.

The point that flow theory didn’t cover is the disconnection between the player’s in-game performance and actual competence. Several studies have shown that regardless of the player’s skill level, while the unaware use of DDA did improve their performance in the game, there’s little evidence of an increased competence rating. In other words, a low-performing (assisted) player may have an improved score when assisted, but they may still come last in the match ranking. Especially in online multiplayer video games, such disconnection creates an illusion that it’s in fact something in the game not the improvements in the player skills that boosted the performance, which again lowers the player trust in the game and jeopardizes the overall user experience: no one wants to play a game that’s running like a black box. This evidence is also supported by another paper on DDA’s impact of making players overconfident, while risking player’s motivation because the use of DDA was not perceived [10].

The second inherent flaw of DDA is the learning process. Every model takes time and data to “learn” how the user is doing in the game, and the realistic problem of running secretly is that once the process of evaluation gets longer than expected or doesn’t produce a good adjustment dataset, it’s likely that the player will notice the unreasonable difficulty and start to doubt the game’s fairness. Also, for players with higher skills, the use of DDA is more likely to make the level harder or their opponents stronger, which in some cases lower’s the user experience: for everyone there’s a threshold of possible skills, if the algorithm fails to realize that, the outcome will not be optimal.

Another solution besides transparency is being optional, in other words, exposing some parameters to the users and/or letting players choose if they want to turn the system on. I am not alone with this choice. An experiment conducted in 2017 showed that while system-oriented DDA and player-oriented DDA (which player can directly control the difficulty) both improved the overall user experience, player-oriented DDA proved to grant the more sense of control than system-oriented DDA [11].
Part 4: Devil's Advocate
The argument over Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment among players is highly controversial. Admittedly, given the heat of researching findings in DDA, there may be finer ways (such as Behaviors Trees and Finite-State Machines to the Dynamic Scripting approach) that can enhance the user experience. The biggest challenge of making the DDA transparent is that it reportedly has a negative impact on player’s autonomy in the game. Also, the question of if any adaptation of DDA can actually boost the user’s competence remains unclear, leaving a negative mood on its usability especially in multiplayer games. My response to this, however, would be to combine other useful tools to compensate for the negative side of DDA, such as user-generated content, or even to use AI teammates to boost the sense of autonomy.
What’s more, it’s not only a user experience problem. DDA’s usage and detail algorithms vary largely from cases to cases, developers to developers, in which in most cases we don’t have exact knowledge on how it works in games. And how do we evaluate the user experience in different games? Studies have shown that user experience evaluation is dependent on multiple variables, how can we decide an algorithm precise enough to weigh the player experience in different games [12]? Or is that even possible?

The last challenge is that designing a player-oriented DDA system is a daunting task no matter what kind of game it’s designed for. Developing such controllable DDA might meet unexpected challenges in terms of complexity, and need further research on more categories of game applications, where excessive tuning and testing will be required to ensure its efficacy. Exposing parameters to users can be out of control, especially in multiplayer games where numerous players compete with each other. This will require the developers to again, consider the side effect of the DDA system before implementing that into the real application. While in the future articles may continue to discuss the effectiveness of DDA, it might be valuable to switch the focus on the real world applications.
Conclusions
Before many people realized, Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment has been a popular topic among AI systems in games. Now that its use gets more controversial, it’s time for the user designers to think twice and try making the use more transparent and optional, which I believe will benefit the relationship between the game and players, and ultimately enhance most user experience.
