Linear Narrative Level (WIP)
In this fictional linear narrative level made for Uncharted 4: A Thief's End, I am exploring methods of implementing storytelling elements through effective level blockouts and other visual elements.

Introduction
Many contemporary games are featuring rich and highly interactive story experiences as one of their core selling points, but that could be quite an intricacy. In a highly collaborative environment like the game industry, being a level designer means implementing understandings of the story with the mixture of tools you possess. I am creating this artifact to explore methods to creating story and emotional experiences, as well as guiding them strongly in the world.

Challenge 1: Visual Storytelling
Level design should contain some principle use of visual psychology as it paves the way for iterations and art creation. That topic could further be broken down into the following three aspects.
shape psychology
Shapes are one of the basic attributes of objects. Successful use of shape, and pattern of shapes could imply certain emotions to players.
color psychology
Colors are natural and easy way to control emotions.

However, as I discussed in my blog, catering to the needs of colorblind people should be an important task for designers and artists as well. When colors don't work as expected, it's necessary to address the use of other visual languages.
space psychology
3D games have an extra axis in space: height, which leads to interesting spaces that could potentially influence the player. To make players nervous we could use claustrophobic surroundings or low walls going close. To make players feel safe, we could use open space and lots of covers, or letting them reach a vantage point.
transferring architecture
When doing this project I looked up many references for Japanese Shinto architecture and layout. They are great points to start with, but to successfully convey stories through the level, it's always important to think about the emotional curves and player interactions and modify the layout so it fits.
Challenge 2: Guidance
contrast
In my opinion, making contrast between objects is the best and strongest way to applying visual guidance. I used lots of contrast tricks to guide the player's lines of sight to ensure the user flow goes as expected.
main player routes
I first fleshed out what routes I suppose most player will choose: in general, they are easy to spot and give an illusion that they're the fastest and safest way to the destination (of course, things happen).
supporting/optional routes
After I figured the main player routes, I would spend some time branching out the newly-made routes for some alternative routes that also lead to the destination. This will offer some freedom for players and serve as a preparation for connecting the optional (collectible) routes to the main routes.

The last thing in this process are optional routes by which the player can find collectibles in game. They are put not too faraway from the main routes but sometimes hidden behind a corner or underwater so getting all of them are still a challenging task to do.
Challenging 3: Difficulty
A great way to keep boredom out of the adventure is to challenge the player at certain points in the level. This not only spices up the experience, but teaches the player the way of a real adventurer.
interaction frequency
As for the basic character-world interactions including jumping, climbing and grappling etc., I wanted to be very careful to keep the exciting pacing of the adventure while not making the process too tedious or repeating. To address the solution, I created a chart where I keep recording the numbers of basic interactions (and the difficulty) in each smaller areas, and as it transpired, it's a much more lucrative approach to manage the pacing than having people running through the whole area again and again.
difficulty curve
As explained in the level design analysis, a good level should be having smooth but increasing levels of difficulty, and a typical example of that will be combat difficulties. There are three major areas that serve mainly as combat areas, and I controlled their difficulty curve by manipulating area scales, enemies, and covers, three of the most important factor that could determine the combat experience. Details are in the next section.
illusions
Sometimes I created illusions: visually misleading the player a path that just did not work. The key part of this is to make sure the player is not hided from the information, instead, give them but in a compelling way that let them oversee the correct path.
Combat Design
This part is dedicated to my thoughts of combat design in a linear-narrative level. The section titles actually followed the chronicle order of my thought process.
defining goals
I split the goals into three aspects: story-wise, gameplay-wise, and emotion-wise. I have prepared a full combat layout document in this link.
traversing routes
When working on larger combat areas, I would flesh out player's possible traversing routes in advance of getting knee-deep in creating specific meshes and putting in enemies.
stealth and patrolling
The nature of this level raised a challenge for me: to design a combat zone that allows both stealth and all-out gunfight experiences, and either of them are exciting in its unique way. My approach to this was to design enemies' AI for patrolling based on the planned traversing routes.
covers
After I got enemies' basics set up, I spend much time iterating the covers that are supposed to enable both combat and stealth. Too many covers will make the level not only too easy but scatted, not having a focal point; too few covers could possibly ruin the experience and are awkward for the artistic feeling as well.
Level Construction
using colors
In my level design project colors are simple but playing important roles in the whole process. In the first version, I painted everything climbable yellow, while making a pure red material for objects that will break from pre-scripted events, even if those events are not implemented yet. This helps to keep the whole scene neat and understandable for playtesters.

Digging into this topic further, considering the role a level designer plays in the team, coloring things is also a visual communication tool betweens disciplines. That's why later I painted the must-go climbing steps yellow while making the optional climbables white - once the color patterns are established in the process, things would be much easier if we want to even excessively iterate them.
debug camera
I used Blueprint to make a simple free-angle debug camera in play mode, making observing the environment in playtest builds much more accessible. To switch between the player and spectator mode, just press 'P'.
Playtesting & Iterating
Change Notes
Iterations have always been a very important part in my level design process. In light of the largeness of this level, I have been inviting people to playtest each large area (four in total) once I finished them, and absorbed some really good insights of how to polish the user experience from both a level designer and producer perspective. map overview
Compared to text explanation, a illustrated map that's self-explanatory would be more friendly.
combat adjustments
After the second playthrough, I was suggested to further clarify the combat layout by providing more details on how the player is supposed to react in each area, and how I could design enemies based on that. As a consequence, I put more blockings and environmental objects in, in an effort to ease out the difficulty curve.

Level Element Analysis
start
The playable character (in this case, Nathan Drake) is spawned at a location relevant to the level destination but away from that. The start scene is very often peaceful, as a nice transition from excitement in the last scene. In Uncharted 4 many levels are continuously using the same maps and goals, where each individual level is designed to control the pacing and the delivery of story pieces.
goal
The goal in a linear story telling level is at its core to advance the story. Here's a breakdown of the goal and storyline in Uncharted 4.
rules
Very diverse gameplay systems may be used throughout the game, but for levels that concentrate on shaping a strong plot, I find more common for them to repeatedly use a reliable and simple interaction system than consistently introducing new and irrelevant mechanics. Here's a list of what Nathan Drake can do in Uncharted 4.
obstacles
What challenges the player from achieving the goal in a linear story telling level? My answer is "the level itself". The level itself is being integrated into a part of the story and inviting the player to indulge themselves in it.
