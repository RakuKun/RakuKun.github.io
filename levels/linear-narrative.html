Stealth Level Design
This stealth level marks as my first work in Unreal Engine. In this project I tried to answer three core questions: What a stealth level flow should be like? How to use visual elements to guide the player? How to design a level with limited interior space?
time summer 2020
length ~ 10 minutes
software Unreal Engine 4, Maya, Illustrator
work individual work
Stealth Design Theory
Stealth game became a serious game genre after Metal Gear Solid came out. Its (mostly seen) third person perspective gameplay, combined with dramatic plot and the thrilling feeling taking down enemies, fascinates players who want something different from a shoot-'em-up.
element analysis
I began the project by analyzing the basic chassis of stealth games. Just like other games, some fundamental elements in stealth games should be underlined: a start, a goal, game rules, and obstacles.
start
Usually the start point in a stealth level is a relatively safe place distant from destination. Being a safe place means players have sufficient time to get prepared such as grabbing some equipments and inspecting the level for a plan. To make the game more challenging, I used the method of backgating: disallowing players to return to the spawn place once they left.
goal
The goals in stealth game feels very diverse but are mechanically the same: to find some fixed target (treasure, exit, VIP, etc.). Some games would ask the players to escape once they finish the first goal, and the game pace could change a lot in the escape phase, adding excitement and new anxiety to the emotion flow.
rules
The principle of designing rules for stealth games is never to make the playable character too strong that they can confront a group of armed enemies like CoD. As a compensation, the character is offered some skills or items to help cope with enemies unnoticed. The enemy AI, which is another important factor in game difficulty, needs to be at a fair level to make their actions reasonable and acceptable.
obstacles
There are usually a group of enemies on certain patrol routes, trying to find and catch the player character. They outnumber the character so it is hard once a combat is triggered. For a playable level the enemies' action must be predictable so the players can have a strategy and plan ahead. On the other hand, players need to use the environment to take cover, so many blocking volumes should be in the level too.
pitfalls
god path
The god path is a route by which the character have zero chance of being noticed. It usually happens when all enemies' patrol routes don't overlap with the character's route. This bad design could make the level very boring as the players don't need to think, they just go and win.

Another example of god path is the unfair vantage point, where players are allowed to attack enemy from distance while they cannot fight back. Again, every game need to balance its challenge and reward. God path avoids all challenges, thus causing a broken game setup.
unpredictable AI
Unpredictable AI means pre-scripted enemies don't follow a fixed path, or have sudden turn back, or moves too fast. Unpredictable AI makes the game hard in an annoying but not challenging way, as the random events would kill every plan and mess up the level.
forced gameplay
Many stealth games let playable character have items or weapons. This is nice as the players are granted a variety of ways to beat the level. But this could also be a pain when some items are designed very specifically for one level or even one point in level, and the players cannot beat the level if they don't have that. It is always better to provide possibility for trying new ways to explore than limiting the gameplay to one answer.
game reference
Splinter Cell: Blacklist
Splinter Cell: Blacklist is the stealth game that I'm mostly getting inspired from, and this stealth level project is like the re-creation of one of its levels called Private Estate. A scenario set at night, Private Estate serves as a great example of the use of light, enemy AI, and guiding players in a typical stealth level.
MGS V: Phantom Pain
Metal Gear Solid V: Phantom Pain is a critically claimed stealth title from one of my favorite game director, Hideo Kojima. I especially like its day-night system, which adds another layer of fun and strategy to the same map. Again, MGSV teaches a good lesson on how crucial lighting in dark scenes.
Uncharted 4
Although Uncharted 4 is not a stealth game, some of its levels cleverly add stealth element to make the game feel diverse and interesting. What's more, the way it tells a continuous story using level design is a great source for learning. The picture above comes from "get out of the orphanage" scenario in chapter 1, which shows the players a segment of Nathan Drake's childhood.

This level did another point extremely well: making wait time interesting. As usually seen in stealth levels, at some points players have to wait till enemies pass by or turn around, and the process can be tedious. Uncharted 4, however, makes NPCs talk and do things all the time, so that players can stay at a high interest level and get useful information to push the story. It's indeed a clever trick.

When designing, I studied pictures of many real world mansions to better understand the mansion layout and design elements. Simply studying the pictures was gratifying.
2d layout
Prior to grayboxing, I used Adobe Illustrator to lay out the map in 2d. Some of the more important things I wanted to clarify in 2d map are:
- determining the critical path;
- double-confirming the goal of the level;
- assuring that the level is well paced.
Process
methodology
macro -> micro
I like to ponder the design from a macro perspective first, then zoom in on the details. For this project, the game pace is the first thing confirmed. When I was drawing the 2d map, I always design the large part first (exterior & interior), then split each part into smaller pieces (garage/living room/bedroom), then decide where to put the lighting, then consider level dressing such as furniture.
3d modelling
Putting 2D maps into a 3D world is not simply replacing lines with cubes. I need to think how to translate the design faithfully and efficiently:
-Pay special attention to the height difference. Height difference would matter a lot in determining the visibility, and engaging enemies from a higher place is possible but is almost impossible from a lower place.
-Lines in 3D world have height and thickness. Wall is a key component in 3D stealth games as it blocks the sight and gives space for players to hide and ambush.
level design
patrol patterns
I've concluded three types of possible enemy patrol patterns, and analyzed how to implement them in levels.
mesh & color
Although the purpose of this level is not to make an environment design portfolio, I built the level in medium detail, especially in places that should attract more attention.

What is interesting is that, when I was blocking out the level and testing, I found in real practices, each floor in interior levels should better be HIGHER than in real life, due to fact that the position of third-person camera is usually higher than the player character, which makes the surroundings look lower.

I paid special attention to the colors being used on meshes. For a level design project, I guess the prior job for color is not really to make a level "real", but to make it MEANINGFUL. Every color should tell the player clearly its purpose: maybe it's to attract attention as a focal point; maybe it's to indicate of a place where special item hides; maybe to convey faithfully a mood of the scene.

Translucent material is another component I played with this time. I used it on pieces of mansion window glass to make the mansion feel artistically modern and alive, but also to help players see through some part of the mansion and guide them. An example is the glass in front of stairs so players can see that the first moment they enter the mansion.
lighting
Lights plays a significant role in scenes at night. In my level lighting was used mostly to indicate important locations and hence direct the players. Lights can also affect the mood of a place: less lighting suggests a potentially dangerous place while bright lighting invigorate people to explore.
collectibles
One of my principles of using collectibles is that I didn't use them to attract players, but to reward those who have curiosity and patience to explore every corner of the world. So they serve as a result of exploration.
level construction
I used Blueprint to do all game logic, and it's also a point where I can watch how level runs and adjust parameters accordingly.
patrol system
enemy AI
To create a highly customizable and robust AI system, I used a combination of Unreal Engine's AI perception system, behavior tree, and various Blueprints. Also to increase the visibility of AI sight, I used enemies' flashlights to indicate their sight range, which proved to be working well in playtests.
I think in a dynamic point of view. Enemies have vision cones and as they move, they are observing different places. As I programmed the whole game system, there are way more details that needs careful thoughts.
interactive system
Having a consistent input system for interaction is essential in eliminating confusions. Players are given the capability of interacting with various objects including enemies, doors, collectibles and intels, for which only two keys (interact, attack) will be required.
dialogue system
Dialogue system in this game also plays as a guide that reminds the player of where they are and what they should be doing.
playtesting
I consider playtesting a crucial part of level design, especially when I realized the magic it plays to bring a 6/10 level to 8 points. (or higher!)
The first feedback from a professional includes making better use of the vantage point and adding more guide along the level so the player can keep track of where they are and where is the destination. I also noticed that enemy patrol routes are so easy that there's actually "god path" which is mentioned earlier as a pitfall in stealth levels. To solve the problem, I re-designed enemies' patrol routes and also created more obstacles.
Post-Mortem
what went well
For the three core questions raised at the beginning of the process, I think I did first two of them well, while still have much to learn.
level flow
Rome is not built in a day.
atmosphere
Almost every playtesters mentioned the intense atmosphere when playing, which quite fits my expectation.
using the engine
Prior to the project (which is my first official UE project), I spent about two weeks learning some basic stuff about Unreal Engine, all of which eventually proved useful. The best part of all these systems is their compatibility - Unreal Engine makes it easy to transplant the systems to another game that needs them.
what didn't go well
interior design
Admittedly, designing indoor level almost took me twice the time of outdoor part. I realized the composition became really different when I wanted the guide the players in a narrow area. And setting obstacles was also more difficult when designing the inside part.
details
Yes, I talked to myself and found that compared to enhancing the details, I was more willing to think about the macro - feeling, level flow, constructing logic... The level was definitely not perfect as I sensed many details are still missing - and some of them might have been a good guide or challenge.
