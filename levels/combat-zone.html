FPS Combat Zone (WIP)
Designing engaging multiplayer FPS combat experiences have alway been a very interesting topic to me. This page covers the process of designing both a symmetrical and an asymmetrical 1v1 FPS combat level using concepts like choke points, covers, lines of sight, and combat distances.
time winter 2020
length 1 minute
software Unreal Engine 4, Maya, Illustrator
work individual work
Inspiration
I was spending quite a bit time during my summer and winter 2020 vacations watching some top streamers on Twitch playing Call of Duty: Warzone (battle royale). Among many interesting game mechanics, the desperate 1v1 gunfight in a fictional gulag attracted me most for its dramatically fast-paced combat and the cruel rule that only the winner can step back into the battlefield again.
System Analysis
what's gulag?
As a Battle Royale game, Call of Duty: Warzone is praised for its fast-paced and the consistent Call-of-Duty feeling. A player is put into the gulag if and only after they get killed in the game for the first time, and the gulag is a chance for them to push back to the main game - if they manage to win their opponents.

A one versus one fight is prepared for everyone in the gulag, and some special rules apply: the system will pick a gun (or not!) randomly for both sides, and/or some equipments such as flash grenades or C4. The health does not regenerate here, and each round only has 40 seconds, with a "overtime" setting where players can also capture the flag in the middle of the map to win.
Here's the link to the official guide to gulag.
why gulag?
The gulag is designed, along with many other unique systems in Warzone, in order that players can MAXIMIZE their playtime in the game and keep excited. Similar to the main game where TTK (time to kill) is relatively short compared to other Battle Royale games, gunfights in gulag also try to make battle short and exciting. The higher the risk, the better the reward.

In the gulag map, many factors work successfully together to make the gunfight fast and intense. I want to discuss how designers achieve their goals by looking into the 2d and 3d layout of the map.
Level Analysis
symmetrical maps: 2d
When Warzone was first released, according to the official guide, the 1v1 gunfight can take place randomly at one of the following three maps.
The three maps do have different layouts.
The first map has the walls on both left and right side of the u-shaped wall offer a great cover when players are trying to engage in a long-range gunfight. However, trying to capture the flag in this map will be a bit scary since the flag is located totally in the open. Crossing the middle lane would be a bold action.
The second map features a circular stall in the middle which could be used to cut down sightlines for a close-range combat. This map is also if the player wants to play more defensively - the rectangular walls offers covers for long-range gunfights.
The third map offers the longest sightlines through a very narrow aisle on top and bottom and the map, making it a great map for long-range fights. On the other side, the blocks in the mioddle also provide the close-range combat option if the players have shotguns.

From 2d layouts we can find the three maps share some things in common:
- Rigidly axial symmetric layouts. The Symmetric map layouts ensure that both sides are in a strictly equal competitive environment, and the win or lose is really only depending on individual skills. However, such maps may be a little bit short in terms of tactical strategies, because both sides ideally will do the same actions and it's really up to their shooting skills.
- Single-floor, small map with lots of obstacles blocking the sight. From the purpose of the Gulag, we know that designers want this combat to be quick and decisive, which pushes the map to be simple and understandable as possible. But being simple does not means being easy: the players should be put into a challenging enough environment where they need to use everything they're provided to win.
- Option for both long-range and close-range combats. In the Gulag the players are given randomly the same pistols/shotguns/rifles etc., and all three map layouts fit most weapons. The blockouts have some long sightlines and at the same time put the flag in the very middle of the map, making capturing the flag probably the most dangerous thingto do there. The flags are more likely to be used as baits if one side wants to trigger a close-range gunfight and put the enemy in a relatively passive position.
- A perimeter balcony above where idle players can observe the ground. No doubt this design gives advantage to the players who have teammates in the same jail. It is a really interesting design to keep players from the waiting boredom that allows some teamworking in an uncommon situation.
symmetrical maps: 3d
One very important design I found about the three gulag map is that, most walls are so TALL that players cannot peek upwards, but have to peek left/right, which adds to the odds they get killed.
This design again forces the players to take higher risks in the fight, by moving closer to the enemy or exposing a larger part of the body.
asymmetrical map
On December 16th, As part of the Season One content from Black Ops Cold War, the game introduced two new maps for the Gulag, including an asymmetrically-designed map: Verdansk. This map is very interesting because it broke the convention of symmetrical maps used for the Gulag, and therefore, the gunfights will be a bit more tactical there. I drew the 2d map above myself according to the 3d map.

routes and choke points
Among all possibilities, there are ultimately three player routes divided by a truck and an empty container with holes, and the players spawn at the opposite corners on the map. The following images describe the choke points in the upper and lower routes.
For the first route: for the blue player, the natural strategy would be going right along the container and peek through the holes, as that's a safer way than to go to the other side of the truck, and the fact that most right-handed players tend to go to the right side. The red player arrives just a bit earlier at the choke point and can pre-aim the blue player. For one-shot weapons like shotgun, the win is really just on who fires earlier. However, peeking through the holes is in a bit favor of the blue player who has just a bit better sight than the red player, making this path balanced with long-range weapons.
The the second route: either side wants to take the risk of traversing all the way along the truck and as the chances are high to meet the opponent in the middle way, which leads to a 50-50 gunfight that's essentially on who aims better, so the engagement there would be from long distance. It will takes some time for the blue player to get to the edge of the truck while the red player can almost aim at the spawn point, so naturally it will be a bit in favor of the red player.
The third route seems to be the most balanced situation where both sides just choose to go middle and take advantage of the covers there while engaged in a mid-range combat. The real problem is, the covers are too low, essentially exposing half of the player's body. In the real world scenario, players would more likely to use the truck/container as their cover and again fight from distance.

There is choke point in each player path. Holes on two sides of the empty container make this area a choke point in this small map. The cars and trucks can turn into choke points in some combat. But at a larger sense, this map is still pretty balanced because the covers and spawn positions are not favoring either side. After all, in the Gulag in Warzone, every kill happens so fast that if one wants to win, they must get their shots in. Having an asymmetrical map definitely adds some freshness to the both sides and sometimes people just like to blame on objective things: "this map is in favor of my opponent and that's why I lost!"

1v1: Symmetrical
2D Map
My standard for designing a small 1v1 gunfight map are mainly twofold:
- to allow both long-range and close-range combats to suit different guns. This means there should be long lanes that offer great sightlines as well as close-range covers.
- to encourage some strategies such as smoke-and-flank, baiting. Additionally, the flag needs to be placed at a obvious but dangerous place, effectively making the capturing process more breathtaking.
